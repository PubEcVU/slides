10.1 Inequality

Inequality of Income, Consumption or Wealth

  • to quantify inequality, good micro data sources are needed
  • consumption: survey data
  • income: income tax registries
  • wealth: limited survey data, limited wealth tax registries

Statistical Distributions

  • PDF, CDF
  • Lognormal PDF
  • measuring inequality is about describing and quantifying inequality of some distribution of a variable of interest (income, wealth, consumption) in statistical terms
  • let \(Y\) denote the variable of interest
  • main idea: take the values \(Y\) (or a random sample) as given and study the aspects of its distribution that tell how ‘different’ people are from one another
  • if \(Y\) is continuously distributed, then its distribution can be represented by
    • a probability density function (pdf) \(f_Y(y)\), or
    • a cumulative distribution function (cdf) \(F_Y(y)=\int_{\min\{y\}}^y f_Y(z) \text{d}z\)
  • lognormal distribution as common tool for modeling distributions of continuous economic variables such as income, consumption and, somewhat less often, wealth
  • a log-normally distributed random variable \(X\) has a natural logarithm \(\ln(X)\) that is normally distributed
  • pdf \(f(x)\) has two parameters, \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), that determine metrics for
    • location (mean, median, mode)
    • spread (standard deviation)
    • shape (skewness)
  • see Wikipedia

Play with the Log Normal Distribution, change \(\mu\) and/or \(\sigma\), and pull the green dot to see the CDF, \(F(b)=\int_{0}^{b}f(x)dx = \Pr(x\le b)\)

Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

  • Description
  • Illustration
  • 2 Income Groups

  • most popular measure for inequality are Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve \(\Lambda\)

  • \(\Lambda\) encompasses all values of the distribution and can be derived from the pdf or cdf

  • starting point are the values of \(Y\) in ascending order

  • determine \(p\), the cumulative percentile of individuals considered (\(p\in [0\%, 100\%]\))

  • \(\Lambda\) measures for each \(p\) the cumulative share of income in total income (\(\Lambda \in [0\%, 100\%]\))

  • \(\Lambda\) typically below the 45\(^\circ\)line, monotonically increasing and convex

  • Gini coefficient of inequality based on cumulative difference between \(\Lambda\) and line of equality

Pigou-Dalton Principle

Under a transfer from a rich to a poor individual that preserves both ranking of individuals and total income, inequality must decrease.

\[\begin{eqnarray*} \mbox{CV}^\dagger &=& \frac{\sigma}{\mu\sqrt{N-1}} \\ \mbox{Gini} &=& \frac{1}{2N^2\mu} \sum_i \sum_j | y_i-y_j| \end{eqnarray*}\]

\[\begin{eqnarray*} \mbox{Theil} &=& \frac{1}{N \ln N} \sum \frac{y_i}{\mu} \ln \frac{y_i}{\mu} \\ ^\ddagger\mbox{Atkinson}_\varepsilon &=& 1 - \left[\frac{1}{N} \sum \left( \frac{y_i}{\mu} \right)^{1-\varepsilon} \right]^{\frac{1}{1-\varepsilon}} \end{eqnarray*}\]

\(^\dagger\)CV: coefficient of variation (\(\neq\) compensating variation) \(^\ddagger\varepsilon\) society’s degree of inequality aversion

  • If SWF suggests that inequality is of policy concern, then redistribution, typically from the rich to the poor, is a possible choice
  • note: such redistribution does not tackle the causes of inequality, rather its symptoms
  • distribution of incomes \(\{y_1,\ldots,y_N\}\)
  • Budget-neutral redistribution: tax everyone using proportional income tax \(\tau\), redistribute total tax revenue \(\sum_i \tau y_i\) as a lump sum to everyone: \[ \mbox{after-tax income } \tilde{y}_i = y_i(1-\tau)+\frac{\tau \sum_i y_i}{N} \]
  • assume no behavioral response, i.e., mean income does not change with redistribution

10.2 Linking Inequality and Welfare

SWF and Incomes

  • SWF
  • Inequality

  • In SWF \(W=W(\color{blue}{u^1},\color{red}{u^2}),\) \(u^1(),u^2()\) are functions of consumptions, \[W=W(\color{blue}{u^1}(x_1,x_2),\color{red}{u^2}(x_1,x_2)),\]

  • however, at given income \(y_1\), consumption demands for individual 1 are \[x^1_1=x^1_1(p_1,p_2,y_1) \qquad \text{and} \qquad x^1_2=x^1_2(p_1,p_2,y_1)\]

  • at those optimal demands, indirect utility is defined as \[v^1(x^1_1(p_1,p_2,\color{blue}{y_1}),x^1_2(p_1,p_2,\color{blue}{y_1}))\equiv v^1(p_1,p_2,\color{blue}{y_1})\]

  • and so \(W\) is a function of individual incomes \[W=W(v^1(p_1,p_2,\color{blue}{y_1}),v^2(p_1,p_2,\color{red}{y_2})) = W(\color{blue}{y_1},\color{red}{y_2})\]

  • social welfare may increase with equality of incomes

  • consider utilitarian SWF \(W=u^1+u^2+\cdots = \sum_h u^h\) and rewrite as \[W=\sum v^h(y_h)\]

  • equally distributed equivalent income \(y_{ede}\): \[\sum_h v^h(y_h) =\sum_h v^h(y_{ede})\]

  • society is willing to give up \(\pi = \mu - y_{ede}\) to eliminate inequality (\(\pi\) inequality premium compare: risk premium)

  • suppose, all \(v^h=v\) (identical): the more curved \(v\), the stronger the effect of unequal incomes on welfare: inequality aversion \(\uparrow\)

Inequality Aversion Illustrated

  • \(E\): given distribution of incomes (endowment)

  • \(Y_\mu\): distribution of incomes with same expected value

  • \(y_{ede}\): equally distributed equivalent income

  • inequality premium \(\pi = \mu - y_{ede}\)

Link SWF and Welfare Indices

  • Marginal SW Weight
  • Intuition
  • Inversion
  • Atkinson

  • compare two income distributions \({\cal A}\) and \({\cal B}\) with \(\mu_{\cal A}=\mu_{\cal B}\), for which \(\Lambda({\cal A}) > \Lambda({\cal B})\)

  • then, if Lorenz curves do not cross, \({\cal A}\) has higher welfare than \({\cal B}\) under every symmetric and concave SWF

    • need not know SWF to say that social welfare is higher under \({\cal A}\)

  • conversely, if Lorenz curves do cross, different rankings can be obtained

    • need to know SWF to say whether social welfare is higher under \({\cal A}\) or under \({\cal B}\)

  • income-based SWF: marginal social welfare weight \(\omega_i = \partial W/\partial v^i\cdot\partial v^i/\partial y_i\) will be rewritten \[\frac{\partial W}{\partial y_i}\]

  • for \(y_1<y_2\), have \[\frac{\partial W}{\partial y_1}\ge \frac{\partial W}{\partial y_2}\] i.e., a dollar extra for the poor counts more, because \(W\) is assumed concave in incomes

  • transfer of 1$ from rich to poor, makes society better off, \(W\uparrow\)

For SWFs based on utilities of income,

  • any inequality measure implies a particular SWF
  • any SWF can be inverted to obtain an inequality measure

  • index \[\mbox{Atkinson}_\varepsilon = 1 - \frac{y_{ede}}{\mu}\]
  • SWF \[W_{Atkinson}(y_1,\ldots,y_N)=\sum_i\frac{y_i^{1-\varepsilon}}{{1-\varepsilon}}\]
  • \(\varepsilon\) degree of inequality aversion

10.3 Poverty

Canonical Poverty Measures

  • Measures
  • FGT Index
  • Graph

Canonical statistical measures of poverty (Poverty Indices, PI)

  • headcount ratio (HC): what percentage of population has income below poverty line?
    • easy to measure and understand
  • poverty gap (PG): what is the average relative distance of the poor from the poverty line?
    • reflects (average) depth of poverty
  • squared PG (SPG): what is the average relative squared distance of the poor from the poverty line?
    • reflects severity of poverty, as it gives the highest weight to the poorest

The Foster-Greer-Thorbecke (FGT) index captures all three PIs \[ FGT_{\alpha }={\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right)^{\alpha } \]

  • \(y_i\) individual income, \(i=1,\ldots,N\)
  • \(z\) poverty line
  • \(N\) number of people
  • \(H\) number of poor (incomes below \(z\))
  • \(\alpha\): definitional parameter, \(\in \{0,1,2\}\)
  • \(\alpha=0\): HC: \(\displaystyle FGT_{0} = \frac{H}{N}\)
  • \(\alpha=1\): PG: \(\displaystyle FGT_{1} = {\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right) = FGT_{0} \cdot \bar{y}_p\)
    • \(\bar{y}_p\) avg income of the poor
  • \(\alpha=2\): SPG: \(\displaystyle FGT_{2} = {\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{i=1}^{H}\left({\frac {z-y_{i}}{z}}\right)^{2}\)
  • the graph shows the three canonical measures
  • for a mathematical expression check the Math tab

Axiomatic Poverty Indices

  • problem: ad-hoc measures may fail to detect poverty or overemphasize it
  • Sen’s concept: starts from desirable properties of poverty measures
    • anonymity: identity of household does not matter
    • weak transfer axiom: poverty decreases with redistribution among the poor
    • monotonicity: poverty declines with rising income of the poor
    • focus axiom: redistribution among the non-poor does not change poverty
  • Sen’s index: combination of measures \[{\cal I}_{Sen} = HC \left[ IGR + (1-IGR) G^P \frac{H}{H+1} \right]\]
    • \(IGR\): income gap ratio \(\displaystyle IGR = \frac{PG/H}{z}\)
    • \(G^P\): Gini among the poor

Poverty Relief Measures and Consumer Choice

  • Tools
  • In-Kind
  • transfers are used to address poverty to targeted and identified individuals
    • in cash or in kind
  • cash transfers can be used to
    • move individuals back to the poverty line in terms of disposable income
      • e.g., in inflationary periods
    • counteract a price hike on specific goods
      • e.g., using the idea of compensating variation
  • in-kind transfers for particular expenditure categories (necessitities)
    • e.g., social/council housing; food stamps/coupons/special debit cards; reduced public transport fares; access to health services
    • in-kind transfers reduce the choice set

  • in-kind benefit is tied to consumption of good \(x_1\)
  • it cannot be (directly) exchanged for good \(x_2\)
    • there is a kink at \(E_2\) on the red BC
    • individual might choose to consume at the kink
  • cash benefit is fully fungible (grey BC), there is no kink, and it might afford higher utility at \(E_3\)

Anti-Poverty Policy and Labor Supply

  • aim: provide consumption floor (poverty line)
    • encouragement to earn own income (self-reliance)
  • wide array of canonical policy tools:
    • universal basic income
    • minimum wage
    • means-tested benefits
    • negative income tax
    • earned-income tax credit
    • benefit notches

Policy can have unintended consequences (e.g., through perverse incentives)

  • labor supply model applying to low earnings worker
  • under many policy alternatives, budget constraints become nonlinear, interacting in complex ways with work attitude (leisure preference)
  • hint: move through walktrough and tabs in tandem
  • Universal Basic Income is an unconditional fixed benefit
    • possibly while removing all other conditional transfers
  • UBI shifts the budget constraint up
    • there may be income effects
    • no substitution effects
  • in practice, UBI is almost never truly universal
  • minimum wage has, as any other wage rate \(w\) change two effects on labor supply
    • an income effect
    • a substitution effect
  • it is possibile labor supply eventually decreases when the income effect dominates the substitution effect at high enough wage rates
  • means-tested benefit should bring individual up to the poverty line
  • but will be cut dollar-for-dollar as recipient’s own means (earnings) rise
  • implies implicit tax rate of 100%
    • 2 kinks in BC, expecially at participation margin where people might stick
    • induced non-participation, and poverty trap
  • Negative income tax has a guaranteed minimum income, irrespective of labor supply
  • then taxes away the benefits at a rate below 100%
  • mitigates somewhat the sharp disincentive to work of a means test if leisure preference not too strong
  • Earned income tax credit has three phases
  1. phase-in: wage subsidy increases \(w\)
  2. flat part: wage subsidy ceased
  3. phase-out: wage tax decreases \(w\)
  • 3 kinks, first one is most salient
    • keeps incentive to work at small number of hours
  • if program eligibility is tied to an earnings ceiling, earning a dollar above the ceiling may mean complete loss of program benefits
  • resulting non-convex ‘notch’ represents a very sharp discontinuity
    • disincentivizes additional labor supply even for those with modest preference for leisure

Layered Welfare Programs and Implicit Taxes

  • Overview
  • US
  • SE
  • NL
  • Integrated welfare systems
    • tax labor income (possibly progressively) and
    • pay targeted, conditional, or means-tested benefits in different types of layered programs
  • means tests and program qualification thresholds may have recipients fall out of a program altogether
    • can imply implicit marginal tax rates of \(\gg 100\%\)
  • additional complexity arises out of multitude of programs at different levels of government (e.g. municipalities, states, central gov’t) leading to fragmentation, intransparency, overlap and coverage gaps, with bureaucracy and stigma leading to discouragement (incomplete take-up)

Summary and Conclusions

Summary

  • Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient as standard statistical tools to quantify inequality
    • there are many others adhering to Pigou Dalton principle (e.g. Theil or Atkinson)
    • in practice, top-1% share or 90/10 ratio are popular
  • redistribution is welfare-enhancing under a convex SWF, and can be achieved using a simple budget-neutral tax-benefit system (assuming no behavioral response)
    • to some degree, there is a link between inequality and SWF
  • poverty measurements are often tied to distributional aspects (relative poverty)
  • poverty policy aims at providing a consumption floor + in-kind provision: targeting and self-selection of the needy + benefit design can dis-/encourage labor supply (participation)
