8 Optimality and Heterogeneity

8.1 Second Best & Optimal Taxation

Second Best

  • Lump Sum
  • Okun’s Leaky Bucket
  • Illustration
  • Implications
  • 2nd theorem: ‘more equity’ (equality) involves lump-sum redistribution
  • lump sum: form of taxation that is not tied to individual’s choices (e.g.: income, consumption, wealth), but only to the identity of the tax payer
    • tax payer cannot influence his tax bill
    • unrealistic; closest example: UK Poll Tax found little political support
    • needs to be heterogeneous since goal is redistribution (some lose, some gain)
    • needs to be based on private preference information
  • reason: do not want substitution effects, as they lead to welfare losses

‘First Theorem of Public Finance’: There is no such thing as a lump-sum tax.

  • i.o.w.: all taxation is distortionary

Consider a redistribution program that takes from the rich and gives to the poor.

[…] the money must be carried from the rich to the poor in a leaky bucket. Some of it will simply disappear in transit, so the poor will not receive all the money that is taken from the rich. […] I want you to decide how much leakage you would accept and still support [this plan]. Suppose 10 percent leaks out […] Should society still make the switch? If 50 percent leaks out? 75 percent? […] Where would you draw the line? Your answer cannot be right or wrong. […] Of course, the leak represents an inefficiency. The inefficiencies of real-world redistribution include the adverse effects on the economic incentives of the rich and the poor, and the administrative costs of tax-collection and transfer programs.
\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Arthur M. Okun, 1975,
\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\) Equality and Efficiency: The Big Tradeoff.

  • efficiency losses shift the UPF inwards
  • second best \(S\): maximize social welfare subject to the constraint that efficiency losses are unavoidable
  • trade-off: equity \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) redistribution \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) taxes \(\uparrow\) \(\Rightarrow\) welfare costs \(\uparrow\)

Implications for the Cost of Public Activity

  • if all taxation is distortionary, then public finance-relevant activities will be associated with the (social) welfare cost of raising taxes

  • these social cost of public funds should be held as small as possible, but are unavoidable

  • denote the marginal social cost of public funds by \(\lambda\), then we say that tax-financing a public project of $1 costs $1+\(\lambda\) in terms of real economic resources

    • \(\lambda=0\) first-best
    • \(\lambda>0\) second-best

Inverse Elasticity Rule

  • Idea
  • Solution
  • Example 1
  • (cont’d.)
  • Example 2
  • many markets & economic activities \(\to\) many possibilities to tax
  • each tax leads to revenue and excess burdens
  • if we add it all up, we get total tax revenue, and a total welfare loss of taxation
  • the total revenue could be a target value of fiscal policy, a revenue requirement to finance public tasks
  • but: we can choose each time how to tax, how much to tax and whom to tax
  • all of this may have an impact on revenue and excess burdens in individual markets
  • so: what’s the ‘optimal’ tax mix that
    • minimizes the total welfare loss
    • while meeting the revenue requirement?

Inverse Elasticity Rule.

The total welfare loss of taxation is being minimized when
tax rates are inversely proportional to the price elasticity of demand
(holding constant the price elasticity of supply).

  • a given tax revenue should be achieved using a tax-mix where commodities/activities with the lowest price elasticities are being taxed most
  • smallest total allocative distortion
  • 2 commodity markets, 2 tax rates: \(\tau_1\), \(\tau_2\)
  • deadweight losses: \(DWL_1(\tau_1)\) and \(DWL_2(\tau_2)\); and tax revenues: \(T_1(\tau_1)\), \(T_2(\tau_2)\)
  • goal: minimize total deadweight loss \(DWL_1+DWL_2\)
    subject to total tax revenue \(T_1+T_2\) being a constant \(R\) (revenue constraint)
  • tax rates are choice variables (policy parameters)
  • technique: Lagrange (with multiplier \(\lambda\))

\[\min_{\tau_1,\tau_2,\lambda} {\cal L} = DWL_1(\tau_1)+DWL_2(\tau_2) + \lambda (R - T_1(\tau_1)-T_2(\tau_2))\]

  • interpretation \(\lambda\): shadow price of tax revenue in terms of efficiency losses

\[\min_{\tau_1,\tau_2,\lambda} {\cal L} = \overbrace{10\tau_1^2}^{DWL_1(\tau_1)}+\overbrace{15\tau_2^2}^{DWL_2(\tau_2)} + \lambda (\overbrace{4.9}^{R} - \overbrace{[8\tau_1-5\tau_1^2]}^{T_1(\tau_1)}-\overbrace{[10\tau_2-7.5\tau_2^2]}^{T_2(\tau_2)})\]

FOCs
\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial \tau_1} &=&20\tau_1-\lambda (8-10\tau_1)=0\\ \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial \tau_2} &=&30\tau_2-\lambda (10-15\tau_2)=0\\ \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial \lambda} &=& 4.9-[8\tau_1-5\tau_1^2]-[10\tau_2-7.5\tau_2^2]=0 \end{aligned}\]

solution: \(\tau^\star_2=0.333\) and \(\tau^\star_1=0.4\)

  • for ad valorem tax (`\(1+t_j\)’), DWL can be written \[DWL_j = \frac{1}{2} t_j^2 \cdot Q_{tj} p_{j}\cdot \eta_j^D,\quad j=1,2\]

  • together with \(T_j = t_j \cdot Q_{tj} p_{j}\), get \[\min_{t_1,t_2,\lambda} {\cal L} = \frac{1}{2} t_1^2 \cdot Q_{t1} p_{1}\cdot \eta_1^D+\frac{1}{2} t_2^2 \cdot Q_{t2} p_{2}\cdot \eta_2^D + \lambda (R-t_1 \cdot Q_{t1} p_{1}-t_2 \cdot Q_{t2} p_{2})\]

  • then the FOCs lead to \(t_1\eta^D_1=\lambda\) and \(t_2\eta^D_2=\lambda\)

  • from which follows \[\frac{t_1}{t_2}=\frac{\eta^D_2}{\eta^D_1}.\]

Further Aspects

  • Critique
  • Equity
  • Social Welfare Weights
  • Ramsey Rule
  • inverse elasticity rule suggests market-specific tax rates
    • heterogeneous taxation is second-best efficient
    • based on Paretian Welfare concept
  • involves complicated, intransparent tax rate system when there are many goods
  • high information and compliance cost for taxpayers
  • simplifies: no direct link between markets, e.g. \(DWL_1(\tau_1)\neq DWL_1(\tau_1,\tau_2)\)
  • Paretian Welfare does not take into account equity considerations
    • e.g., tax immobile tax payers, shift burden to those who will not substitute away
  • maximize SWF, e.g., in two-consumer (\(A\), \(B\)) economy \[W(u^A(q^A_1,q^A_2),u^B(q^B_1,q^B_2))\] subject to the constraint that tax revenue must be collected from both consumers’ demand for all goods
  • commodity demands are \(q^i_j, i=A,B; j=1,2\)
  • explicit use of SWF allows trading off interest between consumers (equity)
  • maximization of SWF, \[\max_{\tau_1,\tau_2} W(u^A(q^A_1(\tau_1),q^A_2(\tau_2)),u^B(q^B_1(\tau_1),q^B_2(\tau_2)))\] will require assessing social welfare weights per individual; e.g. FOC for \(\tau_1\) involve \[\frac{\text{d} W}{\text{d}\tau_1} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial W}{\partial u^A} \cdot \frac{\partial u^A}{\partial q_1^A}}_{\equiv \omega^A_1} \cdot \frac{\partial q_1^A}{\partial \tau_1} + \frac{\partial W}{\partial u^B} \cdot \frac{\partial u^B}{\partial q_1^B} \cdot \frac{\partial q_1^B}{\partial \tau_1} \]
  • \(\omega^A_1\) (and similarly, \(\omega^B_1\)) is a social marginal welfare weight for \(A\); i.e., the value that society puts on providing an additional $ of consumption of good 1 to individual \(A\)
  • note: it is customary to use marg. utility of income for \({\partial u^A}/{\partial q_j^A}\)
  • in more general setting, demand for all goods could depend on prices of all other goods
  • hence, dependence on all tax rates in the system
  • changing a single tax rate has implications through the change of relative prices for all markets
  • without proof (see H&M book for derivation:)

Ramsey Rule.

Set optimal commodity tax rates such that compensated demand for each good is reduced in the same proportion relative to the before-tax position.

  • recall: compensated demand nets out income effects (substitution effects matter for efficiency!)
  • observe: simplest setting abstracts from equity considerations (demand system of a single consumer)

8.2 Sin Taxes

Behavioral Aspects

  • Internalities
  • Psychology
  • Graph
  • Policy
  • internalities are ‘within-person externalities’ – effects of one’s own current behavior on one’s own future welfare, or welfare in a different state of nature
  • mostly relevant in intertemporal contexts that view an individual at different points in time has having ‘different selves’
    • example: the present self may choose to smoke today, even though the future self may want the present self to quit today; this way, the present self causes a spillover effect on the future self (who is less likely to quit tomorrow)
  • policy response can make current measures salient and refer to future consequences
    • example: very high cigarette taxes combined with shock-images

  • economic psychologists emphasize a distinction of utilities

    • decision utility
    • experienced utility

  • decision utility: the utility on which an individual takes actions and which results in observed choices

  • experienced utility: the utility the individual uses to assess actual welfare of a choice made

  • when decision and experience are separated in time, the difference can lead to

    • making a choice now
    • regretting it later

  • under behavioral biases, the two may differ, and individuals may suffer a welfare loss that is similar to that of an externality

  • note: neoclassical approach does not make that distinction of difference selves (time consistency)

  • corollary: revealed preference paradigm – what you want shows in what you do

  • in terms of welfare measurement under demand function
  • blue: observed marginal private benefit: results from decision utility
  • red: true marginal private benefit: results from experienced utility

  • behavioral tendencies change the translation of externalities into welfare losses
    • they can mitigate or exarcerbate the problem by over-/understating relevant (marginal) costs and benefits
    • depends on context
  • behavioral tendencies can also generate externalities directly
  • internalities may compound the analytical complexity
  • consumer heterogeneity big issue: problem of aggregation
  • difficult to make clear-cut policy prescriptions

Obesity Women

Health & Taxes

  • Health
  • Taxes
  • Equity
  • sugar intake associated with severe health consequences
  • type-2 diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular diseases, and cancer, are leading causes of disability and death
  • significant increase in health care costs, lost wages due to illnesses and disability, reduced work productivity, earlier retirement, etc.
  • increasing obesity prevalence is now a bigger problem in low- and middle-income countries than in high-income countries
  • 2015, 1/3 of global population overweight or obese, rapidly increasing prevalence
  • prevalence of overweight or obesity:
    • global rise by 60% since 1990 for preschool-aged children
    • example Mexico: above 40% in older children
  • taxes change behavior
  • specific commodity taxes: target specific commodities (e.g., diesel, gasoline), and constant per unit of the consumed good or substance (e.g., 80c/\(\ell\))
  • also associated with goods of which gov’t wants to discourage consumption, hence the name ‘sin’ tax (e.g., alcohol, nicotine – aka demerit goods)
  • recently: sugar tax, in response to secular rise in sugar consumption and causal links to adverse health behaviors and outcomes
  • if those outcomes affect others (directly or indirectly, e.g., via inflated health insurance premiums), sin taxes are meant to correct externalities
  • if those outcomes affect only the consumer (or a ‘future self’ of that person), sin taxes are meant to correct internalities
  • strong negative association between sugar-sweetened beverage (SSB) consumption and income
  • tax incidence of SSB will fall predominantly on the poor
  • however: some evidence that sin taxes work and reduce consumption
  • policy issue: how to trade off the welfare gains from SSB taxation and the distributional consequences?
  • aspect: both gains and burdens concentrated in similar demographics

Further Considerations

  • Aggregation
  • Welfare Weights
  • Correlations
  • need to aggregate from individual consumer to market level
  • aggregation of externalities across consumers: weigh with difference between marginal welfare weight \(\omega\) and value of public funds \(\lambda\) (since sin tax reduces public revenue distortion: e.g., less sugar consumption – better health – lower public health cost)
  • aggregation of internalities across consumers: weigh with marginal welfare weight \(\omega\) (disregard public funds, as no effect on, e.g., public health cost)

social marginal welfare weights \(\omega\)

  • inequality concern: the poor may have higher weights in a social welfare function
  • incidence: if the poor also consume more sugar, the sin tax falls disproportionately on them
  • if internalities are important, they may at the same time benefit disproportionately
  • externalities: correlation between individual demand elasticity and individual’s contribution to externality
    • possibly positive: consumers
      generating the largest externalities are most responsive to the tax
      • then, externality benefits of sugar tax are larger
  • internalities: negative correlation sugar consumption and income:
    • tax burden regressive
      • but: internality benefits of sugar tax likely progressive

Aspects for Optimal Tax Policy In Practice

  • Welfare
  • Policy
  • Empirics

welfare assessment and optimal taxation depend on

  • whether externalities are present
  • whether internalities are present
  • who pays the taxes and who benefits or is harmed by ex/internalities
  • demand (and supply) elasticities / pass-through
  • preference heterogeneity across the income distribution
  • complementarity/substitutability with untaxed, sugar-based consumption goods
  • leakage effects (e.g., cross-border shopping)

  • optimality suggests: highly complex tax system

  • issue: conflicts with principles of good taxation

    • transparency
    • simplicity
    • universality
    • neutrality

  • to keep it simple (manageable):

    • tax (physical) sugar content
    • apply to broad tax base (not just drinks, not just locally)
  • determining the size of demand elasticities, as well as the extent of internalities, externalities and regressivity is
    • very complicated
    • requires very detailed microeconomic data
    • suitable empirical approaches
  • authors of the study come up with a rough advice of 1.5 cents per ounce (5.4 c/100 g)

Conclusions and Summary

Summary

  • Lump-sum taxes and transfers, as required by the Second Theorem do not exist, hence distortionary taxation allows at most second best social choice (efficiency-equity tradeoff)
  • good tax policy-making is in general complex, but also needs transparency
  • elasticities can be used to guide the choice which activities to tax most
  • yet, equity considerations may suggest that the income position of households should be taken into account
  • taxes can be used in addition to protect consumers from themselves, but normative judgment involved (do policy makers think that consumers are not acting in their own best interest)?
