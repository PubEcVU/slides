  • conversely, many other insurances are provided by the private sector to help consumers facing (large) risks; mostly voluntary, and with many parameters to choose from

5.2 Risk & Expected Utility

Risk Attitudes

  • Fair Bet
  • Risk Attitude
  • Math
  • Special Cases
  • different people have different attitudes to risk: some try to avoid or shun any risk, others don’t care, others enjoy it
  • risk attitude is a preference attribute, expressed by considering the willingness to accept a fair bet \[ \left. \begin{array}{rl} \text{risk averse:} & \text{unwilling} \\ \text{risk neutral:} & \text{indifferent} \\ \text{risk loving:} & \text{willing} \end{array} \right\} \text{to make a fair bet} \]
  • a fair bet is an uncertain prospect with a known set of possible outcomes and a known set of associated probabilities that sum to one (a pure random event); the expected value of the fair bet is zero

risky prospect \(X\) (such as income or wealth–not necessarily a fair bet): random variable with realizations \(x\); utility function \(u(x)\), measuring satisfaction derived from \(x\)

risk attitude is reflected in the shape of the utility function \[ \left. \begin{array}{r} \text{risk averse} \\ \text{risk neutral} \\ \text{risk loving} \end{array} \right\} u \text{ is } \left\{ \begin{array}{l} \text{concave} \\ \text{linear} \\ \text{convex} \end{array} \right. \] for concavity/convexity: consider second derivative of utility

Quantitative measures of risk aversion, due to Kenneth Arrow and John W. Pratt

  • absolute risk aversion \[{\cal A}(X) \equiv - \frac{u''(X)}{u'(X)}\]
    • the decisionmaker considers the absolute exposure to risk (say, the number of dollars that can be invested in a risky as opposed to a safe asset);
  • relative risk aversion \[{\cal R}(X) \equiv - X\cdot\frac{u''(X)}{u'(X)} = X\cdot {\cal A}(X)\]
    • the decisionmaker considers the relative exposure to risk (say, the fraction of wealth invested in a risky as opposed to a safe asset)

utility functions for which risk aversion stays constant with a change of wealth \(X\)

  • CARA: constant absolute risk aversion, parameter \(\alpha\ge 0\) \[u(X) = -\frac{1}{\alpha} \exp(-\alpha \cdot X)\]
  • CRRA: constant relative risk aversion, parameter \(\rho\ge 0\) \[ \begin{eqnarray*} u(X) &=& \frac{X^{1-\rho}-1}{1-\rho},\quad \rho\neq 1 \\ &=& \ln(X), \quad \qquad \! \rho=1 \end{eqnarray*} \]

consider a discrete r.v. \(X\) and \(S\) different possible outcomes with associated probabilities \(\pi_s, s=1,\ldots,S\), then the expected value (E.V.) of \(X\) is \[\text E X = \sum_{s=1}^S \pi_s \cdot x_s\] the expected utility is the E.V. of utility \[\text E u(X) = \sum_{s=1}^S \pi_s \cdot u(x_s)\]

  • expected values are fixed numbers (\(X\) varies, but \(\text E (X)\) or \(\text E u(X)\) does not)
  • risk attitude is reflected in relation between expected utility of \(X\) and utility of \(\text{E}(X)\) \[\begin{array}{rc} \text{risk averse:} & u(\text{E}(X))>\text{E}(u(X)) \\ \text{risk neutral:} & u(\text{E}(X))=\text{E}(u(X)) \\ \text{risk loving:} & u(\text{E}(X))<\text{E}(u(X)) \end{array}\]
  • to a risk averse agent, the utility of the sure thing \(\text E (X)\) is worth more in utility terms than the expected utility of the uncertain prospect \(X\), \(\text{E}(u(X))\)
  • utility at the certainty-equivalent outcome \(x_c\), \(u(x_c)\), equals expected utility of \(X\) \[u(x_c) = \text E u(X)\]
  • apply the inverse transformation on \(u\), \[x_c = u^{-1} (\text E u(X))\]
  • if \(X\) is discrete and all \(S\) values are equally likely \[x_c = u^{-1} \left(\frac{1}{S} \sum_s u(X_s)\right)\]
  • risk averse agents willing to pay risk premium (RP) so as to avoid playing the fair bet
  • risk loving agents have a negative risk premium (they pay to play)
  • risk neutral agents’ RP is zero
  • RP: difference between \(\text E(X)\) and \(x_c\)

5.3 Insurance

Insurance Market

  • Demand for Insurance
  • Contract & Pricing
  • Insurer
  • risk aversion implies there is demand for insurance (positive risk premium)
  • insurance contract: transfers financial risk from insuree to insurer
    • insurance contract: specifies parameters such as
      • premium: price paid by insuree
      • indemnity/coverage: amount insurer pays to insuree in case of loss/damage
      • deductible: insuree’s uninsured amount, ‘own risk’
      • coinsurance: %age of loss borne by the insuree
  • with actuarially fair insurance (‘fair bet’), risk averse agents want to sign insurance contract that obliges them to pay a premium in exchange for having the monetary consequences of a risk fully covered

contract …

  • actuarially fair: premium based purely on probability distribution of insured risk (no administration costs of insurance) = total expected loss divided by number of contracts signed
  • loading: premium also reflects e.g., administration cost of processing insurance claims (load: cost mark-up)
  • full insurance: contract replaces risky prospect \(X\) with its expected value, \(\text{E}(X)\)
    • full insurance covers entire loss, partial insurance does not (indemnity < loss)
  • typically risk neutral
  • signs similar contracts with many people whose individual risks are independent
  • calculates expected loss (possibly including load) as sample average over all contracts
  • under perfect competition in insurance industry, insurer makes zero profit

Insurance Market Problems

  1. macro shocks: if everyone in the insured population gets hit by the same shock, then the funds available for the insurer to pay out will not be sufficient to honor all claims (see US UI claims 3/2020); insurance typically works if insured risks are independent (or at least not strongly positively correlated)
  2. asymmetric information: the insuree knows more about relevant facts than the insurer

public insurance (that cannot go bust) can justify 1., 2. affects all insurers (public or private)

5.4 Asymmetric Information

Examples

  • Adverse Selection
  • Moral Hazard

  • Customer considers signing a Health Insurance contract and takes into account his family’s history of diseases in the decision.

  • important:

  1. at least 2 customer types with inherently different but known loss probabilities (e.g.10% vs 30%)
  2. customer is well-informed and knows their type for sure, insurer only knows the type distribution (e.g., 50:50)
  • Insurer offers one-size-fits-all contract based on average loss probabilities (20%) to any type

adverse selection: the good risks leave the market and only the bad remain, paying high prices for insurance

  • Customer chooses health behavior after signing insurance contract

Two types

  1. insurance cover does not provide incentive to take care: ex-ante moral hazard
  2. insurance cover incentivizes to choose expensive treatment: ex-post moral hazard
  • both adverse selection and moral hazard hard imply positive correlation between being insured and experiencing bad health

Adverse Selection Out of Insurance Pooling Contract

  • pooling: same insurance contract offered to all customers
  • competitive insurance markets where actuarially fair insurance is offered
  • but: individual probabilities to incur losses differ in the population
    • different types: each type has their own loss probability
  • informational asymmetry: customers know their type’s probability but insurer only knows distribution of these probabilities over all types (types are known)
  • customer purchases policy only if premium \(\le\) their expected loss
  • one-size-fits-all premium implies: for some, this is a bad deal
  • low-risk people will not sign the contract and select out of the insured pool
  • premium\(\uparrow\) \(\quad\to\quad\) reinforces adverse selection \(\quad\to\quad\) vicious circle

Adverse Selection in Price-Quantity Diagram

  • Idea
  • Graph
  • Missing Market
  • every consumer buys or does not buy 1 insurance contract; \(Q\): aggregate # contracts
  • loss types: heterogeneity in loss probability
  • preference types: heterogeneity in risk premium (willingness to pay WTP)
  • positive correlation between both: those with highest WTP are most costly to insure
  • downward sloping demand curve for insurance: the first customers have highest WTP, the last ones the lowest
  • marginal cost \(MC\) curve of insurer downward sloping
  • average cost \(AC\) curve of insurer lies above \(MC\) (also downward sloping)
  • break-even point (equilibrium) insurers: \(D=AC\)
  • loading: contract admin costs shift up MC & AC without shifting demand
    • despite risk aversion, some people don’t want insurance
  • market unravelling (\(Q_{\text{eq}} \to 0\); “Death Spiral of Adverse Selection”)
    • depends on exact location of \(MC\) and \(D\)
  • extreme outcome: no insurance in market equilibrium at all
    • Akerlof’s (1970) lemon principle

Moral Hazard

  • assume no (adverse) selection (no heterogeneity in risk probabilities)
    • MC and AC curves are flat and identical
  • MC\(_{unins}\) shows the MC (AC) curve for the case that consumers are uninsured, MC\(_{ins}\) is for the same customers that are insured
    • uniform increase in risk probability due to being insured
    • vertical difference measures equilibrium price response to moral hazard

Social Insurance Design

  • Behavioral
  • Mandate
  • Solidarity
  • Ins. Policy
  • psychological aspects: many people
    • cannot effectively commit to a good plan of action that is in their own best interest
    • have difficulties in correctly assessing probabilities
    • command over insufficient forward-lookingness
  • underinsurance can result
  • mandate (obligation to participate)
  • makes behavioral underinsurance a non-issue
  • may be experienced as paternalistic
  • breaks the vicious death spiral of adverse selection
    • can lead to lower prices, and higher consumer surplus
  • makes those worse off that are forced to pay more than what they value it for
    • countervailing effect on welfare loss
    • need SWF to weigh losers and gainers
  • efficient pricing may entail pricing different risks differentially
    • insurers try to better gauge individual loss probabilities and price accordingly
  • mandated insurance can do without efficient pricing
  • subsidies can undo the effect of loading
Risk Solidarity
  • mitigates differentiation: worse risks are not confronted with the full cost they cause
Income Solidarity
  • links premiums to income, without risk-related reason
  • mandated system helps to redistribute outside the tax system (vertical equity)
  • auditing/assessment of claims
  • partial insurance: use deductibles (‘own risk’), co-payments (fixed amount per claim) or co-insurance (percentage), to limit insurance coverage below full insurance
    • i.e., customer has ‘skin in the game’
  • gives incentives to limit insurance use and thus moral hazard (both ex-post and ex-ante)
  • e.g. social insurance:
    • limit benefit levels or duration of benefits (e.g. UI)
    • reassessment of eligibility (e.g. DI)

Conclusions

Summary

  • Theory
  • Policy
  • expected utility theory models rational decisionmaking under risk
    • known and complete set of outcomes
    • known (or presumed) set of probabilities
  • risk aversion as canonical preference description, positively associated with
    • concavity of utility (\(u''<0\)) and risk premium \(>0\)
  • risk averse individuals have demand for insurance
  • insurance redistributes from the lucky to the not-so-lucky
  • prime insurance market failure surface under asymmetric information
    • adverse selection (in the extreme: ‘death spiral’)
    • moral hazard
  • gov’t regulation or public insurance needs to be justified (market failure)
  • social insurances use mandates to break adverse selection, to enforce solidarity, and to protect people from themselves
  • social insurance systems with mandatory participation:
    • avoid premium-inflating adverse selection
    • insufficient forward-lookingness (paternalism/merit good)
    • however: also insure those that otherwise would not want to be insured
  • social insurances only offer partial insurance (e.g. limited duration and level of benefits) to reduce moral hazard
  • social insurances redistribute in defiance of risk-based efficient pricing
