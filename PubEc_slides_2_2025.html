2.1 Exchange and Efficiency

Exchange & Consumption Edgeworth Box

  • Setting
  • Edgeworth Box
  • Pareto Efficiency
  • Ordinality & Incomparability
  • Limitations
  • consider two consumers \(A\) and \(B\) and two goods \(x\) and \(y\)
  • each consumer has their own utility function \[ u_A(x_A,y_A)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad u_B(x_B,y_B) \]
  • consumers have an endowment each: \(E_A=(x_A^e,y_A^e)\) and \(E_B=(x_B^e,y_B^e)\)
  • total resources of \(x\) and \(y\) available to any consumer are fixed at \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\)
    • whatever is not owned by \(A\), is owned by \(B\): \(E_B=(\bar x - x_A^e,\bar y - y_A^e)\)
  • consumers can exchange (swap) units of \(x\) and \(y\) with each other to improve their own utility, there are neither prices nor (money) income, there is no production

Pareto Preferred

An allocation \({\cal A}\) is Pareto preferred to allocation \({\cal B}\) if by moving from \({\cal B}\) to \({\cal A}\) at least one agent is strictly better off, and no-one else loses (Pareto improvement).

  • i.o.w.: Pareto Improvement = mutual gains from trade can be realized
  • Named after Vilfredo Pareto, 1848–1923.

Pareto Efficient

An allocation is Pareto efficient if there is no allocation which is Pareto preferred to it (Pareto superior)

  • i.o.w.: all possibilities for Pareto improvement are exhausted
  • Edgeworth box: \(MRS_A = MRS_B\) along contract curve (if ICs differentiable and strictly convex to the origin)
  • the level of utility has no intrinsic meaning, except a higher number indicates a higher level of satisfaction or valuation: more is better
  • this is an ordinal concept (order or ranking matters, not degree)
  • from a utility number, we cannot say anything about the absolute satisfaction or valuation
  • also, we cannot compare utilities between individuals
    • two consumers could use different scales to measure their utility
  • ordinality and incomparability is all that is necessary for Pareto efficiency
  • Pareto efficiency is a very conservative welfare concept
    • extreme endowments (one agent owns nothing, the other everything) are Pareto efficient
    • postulate of voluntary agreement to trade implies a single agent can have large power to block trades that benefit many others
    • two Pareto inefficient allocations cannot be ranked
    • no guidance what to choose in face of infinitely many Pareto efficient allocations

2.2 Production and Allocative Efficiency

Extension to Production

  • Beyond Static Endowments
  • Production Possibilities
  • Allocative Efficiency
  • considering the production of goods \(x\) and \(y\), scarcity of inputs to production suggests two things:
    • given input quantities, there is a trade-off between production of \(x\) and production of \(y\)
      • this is embodied in the notion of a production possibilities frontier (PPF), or transformation frontier
    • if inputs become less scarce, production can expand in the direction of \(x\) or \(y\)
      • this shifts out the PPF, but can also enlarge a consumer-Edgeworth box
  • the PPF shows what an economy can maximally produce in terms of multiple outputs (say, \(x\) and \(y\)) when the amount of available input is limited to \(\bar z\), and a choice needs to be made whether to use a given input unit in \(x\) or in \(y\): \(z_x\) or \(z_y\)
  • the slope of the PPF is called the marginal rate of transformation, MRT
  • if production possibilities determine what can physically be produced, we can imagine that the PPF limits the availability of resources \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\) that determine the size of an Edgeworth box
  • The Edgeworth box illustrates the condition for Pareto Efficiency: \(MRS_A = MRS_B\)
  • allocative efficiency is achieved where \(MRS_A = MRS_B = MRT\), i.e., the marginal willingness to give up \(y\) for \(x\) of consumers corresponds to producers’ marginal transformation of \(y\) into \(x\)

2.3 Utility Possibilities and Social Welfare

Utility Possibilities Frontier (UPF)

  • Issue
  • Construction
  • (cont’d.)
  • 2D Utilities
  • 3D Utilities
  • Remark: Shape
  • we now consider the level of utility and ask: what is the highest level of utilities that individuals can achieve in a 2-person economy with given resources?
  • i.o.w.: how much utility can we get out of our stock of consumption goods, at most, if we are willing to redistribute between agents?
    • UPF shows the maximum attainable level of utility for all members of society
    • analysis is: given \(u_A\), what is \(\max u_B\)? (or converse)
  • for convenience, we consider utilities that are always \(\ge 0\) \(\to\) origin of graph at 0
  • if the \(u^j\), \(j=A,B\) functions are ordinal, then UPF will be ordinal as well

  • consider two consumers (\(A,B\)) between which a fixed amount \(\bar c\) of a single consumption good can be divided

  • a unit of the good can be consumed by \(A\) or by \(B\), but not by both at the same time: \(\qquad c_B = \bar c - c_A\)

    • increasing consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(A\) is associated with a decrease in consumption (and, hence, utility) of \(B\)

  • this linkage gives rise to the utility possibilities frontier

  • UPF shows how much utility \(A\) has to give up if a reallocation of the consumption good leads to an increase in utility of \(B\)

  • all points on the UPF boundary are Pareto efficient, since utility of one person can only be improved upon at the expense of utility of another person

  • with 2 consumption goods, we can infer the UPF from the Edgeworth Box

  • UPF corresponds to Pareto efficient points on contract curve, i.e., we consider the levels of the indifference curves at the tangential points

  • example in next tab: drawn for utility functions with one argument \(u(c)\)

  • example in second next tab: drawn for utility functions with two arguments \(u(x,y)\)

    • UPF corresponds to contract curve in Edgeworth box

  • non-concave functions are somewhat harder to capture mathematically (i.e., what our math can show has its limitations)
  • the slope of the UPF has no direct interpretation

  • at initial (relative) prices (=slope of price line), total demand for \(x\) is smaller than the width of the box, \(\bar x\), whereas total demand for \(y\) is larger than the height of the box, \(\bar y\)
    • relative prices need to adjust, \(p_x/p_y\) needs to change

First Theorem of Welfare Economics

Under strict conditions, a (general) competitive market equilibrium is Pareto efficient.

  • ‘general’ refers to a complete set of markets
  • among the strict conditions, need perfect information for all agents, and absence of externalities
  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\implies\) efficiency
  • starting from endowment \(E\), market interaction and market clearing lead to equilibrium market prices that make agents pick allocation \(M\): \(E\to M\)
  • 1st Theorem implies: \(M\) is on the UPF (between \(P\) and \(Q\)) and, thus, Pareto efficient

Social Welfare Function (SWF)

  • SWF
  • Relevance
  • Shape
  • Equity
  • Graph
  • Atkinson
  • all allocations on the UPF are Pareto efficient, but differ in terms of inequality of utilities (consumptions, incomes)
  • SWF: society’s `utility’ function
  • Bergson-Samuelson SWF \(W()\): purely based on individual utilities \[ W=W(u^A,u^B)\]
  • social decisions involve trade-off across groups or individuals
    • interpersonal comparability of utilities necessary when redistributing
  • similar to a utility function, SWF is a modeling device without any direct observable counterpart in the real world
  • in the same way as we can conceive a consumer of reacting to price and income changes as if she was maximizing a utility function, we can conceive of a policymaker as if their choices were guided by an SWF
  • this policymaker is often referred to as social planner
  • important is that SWF can be used to show how to balance off opposing interests against one another, that is, it is useful to model a trade-off between groups in society
  • reflecting recent policy discussion, we can conceive of SWF to capture important dimensions of ‘broad well-being’
  • economics offers little guidance as to what is the ‘correct’ shape of the SWF
  • normative and philosophical considerations may play a role
  • that information has to come from other ways at aggregating individual preferences to a social preference structure
  • we could conceive of a social choice mechanism that determines social preferences in a democracy being determined through an electoral voting process
  • in an autocracy, the preferences of a single individual (dictator) or of elite groups determine what to choose for society

Some Further Rationales for Redistribution

  • Rawls: minimax criterion as natural choice when deciding from ‘behind a veil of ignorance’, and focus on equality of outcomes
  • but: Nozick: society has no command over an income it can redistribute;
    • hence: rather focus on equality of opportunity
    • commodity egalitarianism
    • altruism
    • social stability

three canonical cases obtain for varying degrees of inequality aversion, \(\varepsilon\):

  1. \(\varepsilon=0\): society indifferent to inequality, Bentham or utilitarian
  2. \(0<\varepsilon<\infty\): society averse to inequality, Nash or convex case
  3. \(\varepsilon\to\infty\): society extremely inequality averse, Rawls or maximin case

The SWF depicted in the graph is due to Anthony Atkinson and reads \[ A_\varepsilon(u_A, u_B)= \begin{cases} 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(\frac{1}{2}\left(u_{A}^{1-\varepsilon}+u_{B}^{1-\varepsilon}\right)\right)^{1/(1-\varepsilon)} & \mbox{for}\ 0 \leq \epsilon \neq 1 \\ 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(u_A\cdot u_B\right)^{1/2} & \mbox{for}\ \varepsilon=1\\ 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\min\{u_A,u_B\} & \mbox{for}\ \varepsilon\to \infty \end{cases} \] where \(\varepsilon\) can take any value from 0 upwards and \(\mu\) is a constant.

This flexibility allows to consider a large range of (symmetric and convex) social indifference curves.

Similar to: CES indifference curves for a single consumer.

First Best

  • Combining SWF and UPF allows to determine the optimal social choice: First Best, \(F\).
  • \(F\) may differ when different SWF is applied
  • Technically, UPF acts as a constraint on SWF.
  • This way, we bring together Pareto efficiency (UPF) and society’s preference with respect to equity.

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

Any Pareto efficient outcome can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium, for some distribution of initial endowments.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\impliedby\) efficiency
    • distribution function as additional role of gov’t

Caveat: This needs initial, non-distortionary lump-sum taxes and transfers before market interaction starts.

  • the process underlying the 2nd Theorem is called decentralization (achieving a social goal using decentral market allocation)
  • since the most-preferred (SWF-maximizing) allocation is achievable, equity and efficiency are, in fact, separable
  • lump-sum taxes and transfers are a theoretical benchmarking tool
    • requires: consumers cannot influence the tax bill

Summary

Summary

  • The marginal rate of substitution is a key measure to capture an individual’s willingness to trade (or give up, or pay)
  • Pareto efficiency (equalization of MRS across individuals) is a key measure of societal welfare
  • allocative efficiency can result from an extension to include the production side
  • Pareto efficiency can be illustrated with an Edgeworth box
  • To achieve Pareto efficiency, one can rely on property rights and competitive markets
  • Pareto efficiency does not account for inequality concerns
  • The social welfare function does allow to reflect inequality aversion
  • The first best social choice combines the quest for efficiency with views on equity
  • To achieve the first best, lump-sum taxes and transfers are needed
