9 Income Tax

Introduction

  • income tax major source of gov’t revenue
  • main income sources: labor earnings and capital income
    • earnings are endogenous since labor supply (how much to work) is chosen
    • capital income is endogenous since intertemporal consumption (how much to save) is chosen
  • income tax main policy lever at the equity/efficiency trade-off
  • earnings tax often integrated with social benefits
  • income tax main tool for redistribution; in particular: progressive tax on earnings
  • capital income tax: may distort intertemporal consumption decision

9.1 Labor Supply and Taxes

Income & Substitution Effects of Wage Change

  • Wage Change
  • IE & SE
  • point of departure: standard model of labor supply–leisure/work time allocation of time endowment \(H\), versus consumption/income on the axes
  • What happens to a worker’s labor supply upon a pay rise (wage rate \(w\uparrow\))?
    • \(w\uparrow\) implies time endowment becomes more valuable
    • \(w\uparrow\): budget constraint rotates outward
  • two forces: leisure gets more expensive (substitution effect), or time gets worth more (income effect)

click on walkthrough for the following:

  1. starting from an optimal choice, \(\star\)
  2. an increase in the wage rate, \(w\uparrow\) leads to a rightward rotation of the budget constraint around the time endowment point \(H\) (max hours worked)
  3. a negative compensation payment shifts the budget constraint back to the original (red) indifference curve, point \(\circ\) The substitution effect on leisure \(\star\)\(\to\)\(\circ\), due to opportunity cost of leisure rising, is always \(\le 0\)
  4. if income effect \(\circ\)\(\to\)\(\bullet\) on leisure \(>0\), indivdual may work less (total effect on leisure possibly positive)
  5. if it is negative, may work more (total effect on leisure negative)

Nonlinear Budget Constraints

  • disincentive effects on labor supply, depending on (compensated) elasticities
  • due to progressive taxes and tax credits/allowances, real-world budget restrictions are typically nonlinear
  • nondifferentiability and nonconvexities:
    corners, kinks, holes, jumps and bunching
  • choices may not be unique
  • small tax changes changes can lead to large responses

Labor Supply under Alternative Tax Instruments

  • standard model of labor supply (with time endowment \(H\))
    1. lump sum tax (amount \(T_L\))
    2. proportional (flat) income tax at rate \(t\)
    3. progressive income tax with 2 brackets at rates \(t_1=0\), \(t_2>t\)
      • leads to piecewise-linear budget constraint with kink at the bracket treshold (earnings level \(w\cdot h_1\))
  • exercise: compare different tax systems, holding after-tax welfare levels constant
  • after-tax wage earnings
    1. \(w \cdot h - T_L\)
    2. \((1-t) \cdot w \cdot h\)
    3. \(w \cdot h_1 + (1-t_2) \cdot w [h-h_1]\)

Progressive Taxation

  • two ways of measuring progressivity
    • marginal tax rates monotonically increasing
    • average tax rates monotonically increasing
  • often, more complex tax structure arises, e.g., piece-wise linear budget constraint

Flat Tax and Redistribution

  • progressive systems are a good redistributional tool
  • but also have large welfare losses (excess burdens)
  • flat tax may seem to redistribute less
  • however, overall progressivity of the tax-and-benefit system matters
  • i.e., with a flat tax and benefits targeted at the poor, redistributional goals can be achieved
  • also likely to limit welfare losses
  • administrative simplicity and transparency

9.2 Optimal Income Taxation

Incentive Compatible Income Tax Design

  • Idea
  • Setup I
  • Setup II
  • Setup III
  • Video
  • Findings
  • suppose differences in labor incomes are due to differences in productivity, which ultimately are due to differences in talents
  • first best: (lump-sum) tax based on talents or skills / endowment of innate abilities
  • Mirrlees: assume talents are unobservable to the gov’t
  • incentive for the smart guys to pretend they’re not smart (and thus pay lower taxes)
  • second best solution: max social welfare subject to balanced budget, and taking into account incentives to reveal one’s talent
  • recall: truth-telling incentives in design of public good preference revelation mechanism
  • consider 2 types of agents: high and low ability, (\(h\), \(\ell\))
  • asymmetric info: gov’t cannot tell whether an individual is \(h\) or \(\ell\)
  • main issue: \(h\) worker may pretend to be \(\ell\) to pay lower taxes (but \(\ell\) will not pretend to be \(h\) because lower skills make earning higher income very costly)

picture:

  • 2 dimensions: before-tax income and after-tax consumption on axes
    • note: horizontal axes (gross earnings: \(w\cdot h\)) differs from consumption-labor supply diagram (leisure: \(H-h\))
  • agent monotonicity: for a given combination of before-tax income and consumption, \(h\) have flatter indifference curves because it takes them lower effort cost to generate a given level of income than \(\ell\)
  • tax function: vertical distance from 45\(^\circ\)-line reflects tax payment; slope = marginal tax rate
    • note: both agents must face the same tax function
  • design problem: how to optimally set marginal tax rates so that social welfare is maximized, and incomes can be redistributed, without giving \(h\) incentive to masquerade as \(\ell\)?
  • marginal tax rate cannot exceed 100%, because with convex indifference curves noone would choose to be on a downward sloping segment of a tax function
  • marginal tax rate cannot be \(<0\) as SWF-improvement possible
  • marginal tax rate cannot be \(\neq 0\) for \(h\)

  • if talents determine wages, then highest parts of wage income should optimally be taxed at marginal rate zero
  • this maximizes social welfare with a balanced budget
  • result: self-selection
    • it incentivizes highest skill level workers to display maximal effort
    • low skill workers would find this level of effort provision too costly
  • note: this does not mean
    • the marginal tax rate should be zero everywhere
    • the average tax rate should be zero
  • yet, somewhat counterintuitive and not directly observed in practice
    • empirically, the (very) rich pay (substantially) less in many countries
    • average income tax, not earnings tax (see Zucman report)

Additional Remarks

  • Design Literature
  • Median Voter Results
  • Mirrlees (1971) result helped establish new literature on asymmetric information, incentives, and mechanism design
  • important example of ‘’no-distortion-at-the-top’’ principle
  • designing correct incentive structure has many other applications in economics
  • for actual tax design of limited concrete practical importance, however
    • instead offers a benchmark and intellectual framework
  • in political process, tax rates are endogenous (chosen)
  • with a simple flat tax system with lump-sum transfers, the poorer stand to gain and the richer stand to lose from redistribution
  • in real-world income distributions median\(<\)mean
  • if median voter is relatively poor, political equilibria support redistribution through high tax progressive rates

9.3 Savings and Taxes

Savings and Capital Income

  • Capital income
  • NL (‘box 3’)
  • Illustration
  • under personal income tax laws, consumers pay tax on capital income received
  • this may come in various forms, e.g.:
  • dividends (firm’s profits received by shareholders)
  • rent (lease) received
  • capital gains (change of wealth due to asset price changes)
    • realized capital gains: difference between price when actually sold and price when actually bought [US]
    • unrealized capital gains: difference between valuation at time \(t\) and at time \(t-1\) (sold or not)
  • interest receipts on savings
    • possibly minus interest payments on debt
  • 2001-2016: 30% on fictitious return of 4% (=1.2% on wealth)
  • from 2017: ‘double fictitious’ and implicit progressive (nominal tax rate 30%)
    • two fictitious rates of return: savings (1.63%) and investments (5.39%)
    • fictitious investment composition by wealth bracket:
wealth bracket lower limit upper limit assumed composition
1 0 75000 33% invest, 67% save
2 75000 975000 79% invest, 21% save
3 975000 \(\infty\) 100% invest, 0% save
  • 2021: Supreme Court ruling demands taxation of actual returns by 2027

  1. starting from intertemporal distribution of income endowment \(Y\), intertemporal budget contraint of slope \(-(1+r)\)
  2. consumption smoothing leads to choice \(C\) and implies saving or borrowing, depending on relative position between \(Y\) and \(C\)
  3. symmetric taxation of interest income at rate \(t\) leads to net-of-tax returns \((1+r(1-t))\) (orange line)

  5. asymmetric taxation of interest income: most common case: no interest payment deductibility from income tax, while interest income is taxed (differential treatment saving/borrowing)
  6. result of asymmetry depends on whether taxpayer is saver or borrower

Conclusions and Summary

Summary

  • earnings taxes have both substitution and income effects, but smaller DWL under flat tax
  • progressive income tax often used for stronger redistributional aims
  • but even under flat tax, redistribution is possible
  • optimal income tax: no-distortion-at-the-top suggests zero top marginal rate, to
    • keep incentive for very talented people to work a lot and earn a lot
    • this way, generate revenue for redistribution
  • capital taxation on savings/interest for consumers may have both substitution and income effects, and can affect consumer wealth accumulation dynamically
