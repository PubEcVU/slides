4.2 Free Riding

Social Dilemmas

  • Overview
  • Inaction, I
  • Inaction, II

  • public good provision is akin to social dilemmas known from other social sciences (bystander effect, diffusion of responsibility, tragedy of the commons)

  • in this context, inaction is individually rational: action means cost of effort for yourself and sharing results, inaction means no cost of effort and also sharing results

  • free riding: fundamental problem to rely on voluntary action

  • prisoner’s dilemma: equilibrium (market outcome) may result in inaction of all players, which is socially inefficient (underprovision and collective irrationality)

Prisoner’s Dilemma

payoffs: abating costs 7 to the abater, and brings benefits of 5 to every player including oneself

  • two rational countries will not by themselves adopt tighter pollution standards (‘pollute’ is dominant strategy for each)

Extension: Continuous Choice

  • Discrete Choice
  • Continuous Choice
  • (cont’d.)
  • Canonical PD only has 2 actions per player
  • What if there are more?
    • add a row / column for every action
    • \(\#\) rows / columns \(\to \infty\): need a different approach

  • illustration of continuous strategy

  • idea similar to Cournot strategies in oligopoly settings

  • consider two individuals \(i=A,B\), each consuming private \(x\) and public good \(G\)

  • both with income \(m=1\), price \(p_x=1\)

  • budget \(\quad 1 = m_i =x_i+g_i, \quad i=A,B\quad\) and \(\quad G=g_A+g_B\)

  • Cobb Douglas utility \[\qquad u_i=\sqrt{{x_i}\cdot G}\]

    • so that, e.g., \(\qquad\qquad\qquad u_A=\sqrt{(1-g_A)(g_A+g_B)}\)
  • FOC maximization for \(A\): \[ \frac{1}{2} ((1-g_A)(g_A+g_B))^{-\frac{1}{2}}\color{blue}{(1-2g_A-g_B)} =0 \]
  • thus, best response \[\color{blue}{1-2g_A-g_B} =0\quad \Leftrightarrow \quad g_A(g_B)=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}g_B\]
  • symmetry, best response for \(B\): \[g_B(g_A)=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}g_A\]
  • equilibrium: \(g_A(g_B)=g_B(g_A)\): \[g_A^\circ = g_B^\circ = \frac{1}{3}\]
  • total quantity public good \[G^\circ=\frac{2}{3}\]
  • private good \[x_i^\circ=\frac{2}{3}, \quad \sum_i x_i^\circ=\frac{4}{3}\]

Samuelson: Optimal Provision of Public Goods

  • Vertical Aggregation
  • Allocative Efficiency

  • demand aggregated across consumers is
    • private good: horizontal
    • public good: vertical sum of individual demand func’s
  • for two individuals \(A\) and \(B\)
    • private good \(x\) \[\mathit{MC}_{x} = \mathit{MB}_{x}^A = \mathit{MB}_{x}^B\]
    • public good \(G\) \[\mathit{MC}_{G} = \mathit{MB}_{G}^A + \mathit{MB}_{G}^B\]
  • the latter is the simplified form of the Samuelson condition

Samuelson Condition with a private good \(x\) and a public good \(G\):

\[MRS_A + MRS_B = MRT\]

recall:

  • MRS – willingness to give up some \(x\) when getting 1 marginal unit of \(G\) (slope of indifference curve)
  • MRT – units of \(x\) to relinquish when needing to produce 1 extra unit of \(G\) (slope of production possibilities frontier)
  • private goods: \(MRS_A = MRS_B = MRT\)

Pareto Efficient Provision in the Continuous Choice Example

  • Setup
  • Solution
  • social planner’s problem \[\max_{x_A,x_B,G} W \quad \text{s.t.} \quad (x_A+x_B+G-2)\]
    • e.g., utilitarian SWF \(\quad W=u_A+u_B\) and Cobb Douglas utility \(\qquad u_i=\sqrt{{x_i}\cdot G}\)
  • Lagrange function \[\max_{x_A,x_B,G,\mu}{\cal L}= \left(x_A^{\frac{1}{2}}+x_B^{\frac{1}{2}}\right)\cdot G^{\frac{1}{2}}-\mu(x_A+x_B+G-2)\]
  • first two FOCs \(\qquad \displaystyle \frac{\partial {\cal L}}{\partial x_i} = \frac{1}{2} x_i^{-\frac{1}{2}} G^{\frac{1}{2}}-\mu=0,\quad i=A,B\quad\) imply \(\quad x_A=x_B\equiv x\)
  • BC implies \(x=1-0.5G\)
  • then, \(W=2\left[G- \frac{1}{2} G^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]^{\frac{1}{2}}\)
  • FOC wrt \(G\): \[\left[G- \frac{1}{2} G^{\frac{1}{2}}\right]^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot (1-G)=0\]
  • implies \(G^\star=1\), so \(x_i^\star=0.5\)
  • note: \(g_i\) are indeterminate, i.e., infinitely many Pareto efficient solutions
  • \(W^\star = \sqrt{2} > W^\circ = \frac{2}{3}\cdot\sqrt{2}\)

Lindahl Equilibrium

  • Overview
  • Calculation
  • Strategic Behavior
  • Lindahl’s question: what prices would support the efficient solution \(G^\star\)?
  • if we allow preferences to differ, then in order for all consumers to demand the same quantity of the public good, prices or financing shares (or fees, contributions, taxes) may have to vary across consumers: \[\tau_i, \qquad i=A,B\] such that \[G_i^\star(\tau_i)=G^\star = \text{const.}\]
  • these optimal, but heterogeneous financing shares \(\{\tau_A^\star, \tau_B^\star \}\) are also known as Lindahl prices
  • they could be found by a pricemaker calling out sets of prices until all consumers agree
  • we can calculate them under the assumption that consumers don’t act strategically
  • continue Cobb Douglas example, keeping income \(m=1\) and \(p_x=1\), but vary \(p_G\) by varying financing shares \(\tau\)
  • utility for \(i=A,B\): \[u_i(\tau_i,G) = \sqrt{(1-\tau_i \cdot G)\cdot G}\]
  • note that \(\tau_A = 1-\tau_B\), i.e., financing shares add up to \(1\)
  • e.g., for \(B\), FOC: \[\frac{\partial u_B}{\partial \tau_B} = \frac{1}{2}\left((1-\tau_B \cdot G_B)\cdot G_B\right)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\cdot \color{blue}{(1-2\tau_B\cdot G_B)} = 0\]
  • this can only be true if \(\color{blue}{(1-2\tau_B\cdot G_B)} = 0\), or \[G_B=\frac{1}{2\tau_B}\]
  • similarly, we obtain \[G_A=\frac{1}{2(1-\tau_B)}\]
  • we can view \(G_A(\tau)\) and \(G_B(\tau)\) again as best response functions
  • Lindahl equilibrium is where they coincide \(G_A(\tau_B^\star)=G_B(\tau_B^\star)\): \[(\tau_A^\star,\tau_B^\star,G^\star)= (0.5, 0.5, 1)\]

Exercise: show this

  • what, if \(B\) always chooses his best response \(G_B(\tau)\), but \(A\) does not?

  • \(A\) could \[\max_{\tau_A} u_A(\tau_A, G) \quad\text{s.t.}\quad G_A=G_B(1-\tau_A)\]

  • equilibrium then is \[(\tau_A^s,\tau_B^s, G^s)=\left( \frac{1}{3},\frac{2}{3},\frac{3}{4}\right)\] (underprovision)

Exercise: show this

4.4 Mechanisms and Voting

Main Issue to Address

  • the Lindahl result may come about as voting equilibrium

  • individuals voluntarily choose the efficient amount, after having revealed their willingness-to-pay schedule

  • both market and Lindahl solution fail to be optimal because of non-excludability: players have incentive to misrepresent their preferences for public goods if contributions are linked to stated preferences for those goods

  • after all, players can consume (an inefficiently large amount) of the good ‘for free’

  • main informational problem: social planner (gov’t) does not know true preferences of people

  • alternatives

    1. remove non-excludability: turn public into club good

    2. preference revelation mechanism

    3. vote over budget and finance public good from general tax funds

Simplified Preference Revelation Mechanism

  • Intro
  • Example Payment Mechanism
  • (cont’d.)

  • mechanism design: devise incentive scheme that makes it in individuals’ best interest to reveal their true preferences

  • main idea: individuals are being charged according to their influencing the social decision whether or not to buy the good

  • government may buy and pay for a public good of fixed size, and charge the players according to what they say

  • 2 players: total valuation \(\ge\) total cost

  • total cost of provision \(10\), true valuation per player \(8\)

  • players reports either ‘8’ or ‘0’, i.e. are allowed to lie

  • player is pivotal (influential) if his report changes the social decision

player argues:

  • if I’m pivotal,
    and if there’s a possibility that we jointly could afford to buy the good,
    ‘I pay the full cost minus what you report’ (else I don’t pay)

  • my payment does not depend on my own report

  • outcome: truth-telling weakly dominant strategy for both

  • problem: total payments received 4 \(<\) total cost \(10\)

Vote over Size of Public Good

  • Median Voter Theorem
  • Illustration
  • Inefficient(?) Outcome

  • let citizens vote over size of public good \(G\) (or size of gov’t), knowing fully well that more spending on it implies less spending on other (private) goods

  • different people will make different choices \(\to\) distribution of preferred choices

Median Voter Theorem

In election (political equilibrium), the proposal that coincides with the preference of the median voter determines the size of the public good that will be chosen (since it gets at least 50% support of all votes).

  • politically determined size \(G^{MV}\) may be larger or smaller than, or even coincide with, the optimal size \(G^\star\)
  • stable distribution of preferences \(\to\) stability of voting outcome
  • MV result not subject to strategic manipulation

Summary

Summary

  • public goods are important ingredients of a functioning and complex society, and first-order relevant for the organization of gov’t (e.g., legal system, law enforcement, defense)
  • applications extend to many domains, e.g., development and environmen: how can countries coordinate on reducing carbon emissions, when it is cheaper to free ride?
  • other global challenges also reflect the need for international cooperation on international public goods
  • market outcomes (uncoordinated interaction of private agents) often leads to underprovision relative to the efficient level
  • efficiency characterized by Samuelson condition
  • voting (+ tax financing) as practical way to deal with it
