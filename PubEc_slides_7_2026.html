7 Commodity Taxation & Subsidization

7.1 Introduction

Introduction Taxation

  • important purpose: finance gov’t expenditure (tax revenue)

  • intended and unintended allocative and distributive effects

    • intended allocative: Pigou tax, ‘sin’ tax
    • unintended allocative: excess burden
    • intended distributive: income redistribution
    • unintended(?) distributive: tax incidence
    • joint with expenditure: macroeconomic effects of stabilization

  • optimal taxation: given a certain tax revenue goal of gov’t, how to minimize the welfare loss from taxation?

  • derive ‘optimal tax rules’

  • in what follows: consider market for one or two commodities (prices, quantities)

Taxes and Their Market Consequences

  • Tax Distortion: Intuition
  • Aspects
  • Results

consumer: yes-no decision of buying 1 apple

  • willing to pay: 1.20 EUR
  • shop: willing to sell at 1.00 EUR
    \(\to\) economically useful transaction proceeds, welfare gain
  • tax revenue: 0

enter the gov’t: tax 25%


  • at 1.25 EUR, consumer does not buy, shop does not sell
  • transaction stalled, no welfare gain
  • tax revenue: 0

\(\to\) same tax revenue, no welfare gain

  • commodity taxation targets consumption goods
  • absent lump-sum taxes, all taxation is distortionary, implying tax wedge:
    price consumers pay \(p^c\) differs from price producers obtain \(p^p\)
  • distinguish two core forms of commodity taxation
    • ad valorem (or, sales) tax: a percentage \(t\) on price-weighted volume of trade
      • price \(p^c_t = p^p_t \cdot (1+t)\)
    • unit (or, specific) tax: a money amount \(\tau\) added to the price
      • price \(p^c_\tau = p^p_\tau +\tau\)
  • deadweight loss (DWL; or excess burden): welfare loss from reduced economic activity
  • tax incidence: question of pass-through

  • consequences depend primarily on price (or tax rate) elasticities of demand and supply

  • economic incidence \(\neq\) statutory or legal incidence

  • neutrality results

    • form of taxation (ad valorem/sales or unit/specific tax) does not matter (given tax revenue)
    • effects of tax rate changes are symmetric for tax increase and decreases
    • same analytical framework for subsidies (negative taxes)

  • differences according to market structure

  • distinguish introduction vs increasing of tax

7.2 Partial Equilibrium Market-Level Analysis

Incidence

  • burden falls on both producers and consumers according to relative price elasticities

Elasticities

\[ \eta^D = \frac{\mbox{d} D(p)}{\mbox{d} p}\cdot \frac{p}{Q}; \qquad \eta^S = \frac{\mbox{d} S(p)}{\mbox{d} p}\cdot \frac{p}{Q} \]

Core Result

less elastic side of the market is more exposed to the tax-induced price change \[\frac{\text d p^p_\tau/\text d \tau}{\text d p^c_\tau/\text d \tau} = \frac{\eta^D}{\eta^S}\]

Base Diagrams For Competitive Markets

  • Unit tax
  • Subsidy
  • Ad Valorem Tax
  • Elasticity
  • Laffer Curve
  • unit tax levies \(\tau\) dollars per unit of the good
  • parallel shift of demand or supply (marginal cost) curve
  • tax rate change affects
    • tax wedge \(p^p_\tau \neq p^c_\tau\)
    • tax revenue \(T(\tau)\) (green)
    • and deadweight loss \(DWL(\tau)\) (red; aka Harberger triangle)
  • subsidies are negative taxes
  • producer \(p^p_\tau\) and consumer price \(p^c_\tau\) swap position compared to tax
  • DWL obtains because change in consumer and producer surplus does not compensate fully for the amount of subsidy paid by gov’t
  • ad valorem taxes multiply the willingness to pay or marginal cost with a factor \(1+t\)
  • sales taxes are typical ad valorem taxes (percentage on the (sales) value)
  • rotation of demand or supply (marginal cost) curve around the intersection with quantity axis
  • define price elasticities of demand and supply in before-tax equilibrium \(({p_0},{Q_0})\)
  • relative consumer and producer prices change inversely proportionally to elasticities when tax rate changes
  • diagram generated from market demand and supply curves \(Q=D(p)=1-p\) and \(Q=S(p)=p\) at unit tax (rate \(\tau\))
  • both tax revenue \(T(\tau)\) and \(DWL(\tau)\) are quadratic in \(\tau\)
  • \(\tau=0.8\) delivers the same tax revenue as \(\tau=0.2\) but is associated with a DWL that is 32 times higher

Deadweight Loss and Elasticities

  • change in DWL depends on elasticities and tax rate as follows: \[ \Delta DWL = 0.5 \cdot (\text d \tau)^2 \cdot \color{blue}{\frac{\color{black}{\eta^D} \eta^S}{\eta^S - \eta^D}}\cdot Q_0/p_0 \tag{1}\]
    • DWL rises more than proportionally with the tax rate (quadratic)
  • for introduction of a tax, i.e., \(\text d \tau = \tau\), and under a flat supply curve1 \[ DWL = 0.5 \cdot \tau^2 \cdot \eta^D \cdot Q_0/p_0 \tag{2}\]
    • DWL quadratic in tax rate and proportional to price elasticity of demand

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{\eta^S \to \infty} \color{blue}{\frac{\eta^S}{\eta^S-\eta^D}} = 1\)

Base Diagrams For Monopoly

  • there is an initial DWL of monopoly in the no-tax equilibrium
  • tax-induced shift in demand induces production where \(MC(Q_\tau)=MR(Q_\tau)\), and a price wedge \(p_\tau^c\) v \(p_\tau^p\), with smaller profit
  • under taxation, the DWL will grow much more, since an existing DWL will be supplemented with a tax-DWL (distortion-upon-distortion)
  • for unit tax at rate \(\tau\), \[ DWL = 0.5\cdot \tau^2\cdot \eta^D_p \frac{Q_0}{(\eta^D_p-1)^2} \tag{3}\]

Tax Shifting

  • in noncompetitive market structures, tax shifting as a statutory tax payer passing through a tax burden to the other side of the market cannot necessarily be characterized through a simple comparison of elasticities
  • for instance, if the monopolist were the statutory taxpayer, some, but not all of the burden, would be shifted to the consumers
  • tax incidence: firm carries positive tax burden, despite \(MC=0\) (undershifting)
  • depending on the shape of the demand function, overshifting may occur (consumer price rises much more with the tax than producer price)

7.3 Consumer-Level Analysis with Two Markets

Consumer-Level Analysis

  • consumer choice diagram, on axes: quantities of 2 goods, \(x\) and \(y\); only \(x\) will be taxed
  • partial taxes are distortionary because of substitution effects:
    \(\Delta\)tax rates \(\to\) \(\Delta\)relative prices \(\to\) behavioral response
  • comparison: non-distortionary (or lump-sum) tax, holding constant utility level1 2

Excess Burden at Individual Level

  1. foregone tax revenue due to partial taxation, or
  2. tax burden that is higher than necessary

  1. disregarding supply side

  2. concepts involve equivalent (EV) or compensating variation (CV)

Foregone Tax Revenue due to Partial Taxation

Excess Burden (Case 1)

Tax revenue not received due to distortionary taxation. Using non-distortionary taxes would have led to more tax revenue, holding constant the welfare level of the individual.

  • based on EV (compares budgets on after-tax indifference curve)

Tax Burden that is higher than Necessary

Excess Burden (Case 2)

Extra amount of money that the government would have had to spend if it were to restore welfare by means of a non-distortionary subsidy when the relative prices were determined by the distortionary tax.

  • based on CV (compares budgets on before-tax indifference curve)

Lessons Learned

  • partial taxes are distortionary since they induce a change of behavior:
    do a thing you otherwise would not have, even though you’re being compensated for the change in purchasing power (income effect)
  • compensated demand function matters for evaluation of welfare effects
    • substitution effect decisive for efficiency, even if counterbalanced by an income effect of the same size
    • no excess burden if goods are perfect complements

7.4 Welfare-Enhancing Taxes

Externalities Illustrated

  • Definition
  • Graph

Externality

effect of own economic activity on somebody else’s without compensation payments that are accounted for in market prices

  • externalities are essentially unintended spillover effects
  • affect non-market participants (or ‘bystanders’)
  • externalities can
    • be negative, positive, and/or interdependent
    • affect production and/or consumption
  • here: firm pollutes while producing, consumers (as bystanders) suffer marginal damages from the pollution—which increases with production
  • rational behavior: do not take into account social cost/benefits, only private cost/benefits
  • in market equilibrium: \[\text{marg. private cost} = \text{marg. private benefits}\] will have too much of a negative externality
  • policy solutions try to restore optimal allocation: \[\text{marg. social cost} = \text{marg. social benefits}\]
  • this does not imply: abolish all activity with externalities; even at optimal allocation there will be some level of externality present

Policy: Pigou Tax

  • Idea
  • Graph

Pigovian Tax

Tax per unit on a polluting producer’s output equal to the marginal damage it causes at the socially optimal level of production. Concept named after A.C. Pigou (1877–1959).

  • i.e., introduce a price on the externality that the polluter has to pay
  • align marginal social cost of society and marginal private cost of the polluter

  • main problem: implementation
    • get polluters and pollutees to truthfully reveal their private cost and benefits so that taxes can be set optimally
  • Optimal tax increases MPC and reduces production such that the welfare loss from the externality is exactly removed
  • Optimal abatement subsidy increases MPC accordingly and is paid on pollution reduction exceeding a prespecified benchmark (i.e., marginal value of not abating carries a cost of foregone subsidy)

Conclusions and Summary

Summary

  • taxes are the main financing tool for gov’t expenditure
  • they have desirable, but also undesirable consequences
  • tax incidence: the relative inelastic side of the market bears the brunt of the tax burden
  • all taxation is distortionary
  • excess burdens are quadratic in tax rates
    • size of DWL depends on price elasticities of supply and (compensated) demand
    • form of taxation (ad valorem or unit tax) does not matter (given tax revenue)
    • effects of tax rate changes are symmetric for tax increases and decreases
  • in noncompetitive market structures, taxes compound the welfare loss of pre-existing distortions
  • substitution effects are the underlying causes of the inefficiencies
  • welfare-enhancing taxation can result when tackling externalities
