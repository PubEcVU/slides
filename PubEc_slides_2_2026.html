2.1 Overview

The Promise of Competitive Markets and the State

  • Markets

  • markets will not in themselves reflect society’s preference regarding equity

The Two Fundamental Theorems of Welfare Economics

  1. Competitive market equilibria are Pareto efficient.
  2. Any Pareto efficient allocation can be implemented as market equilibrium when the state redistributes initial endowments.

2.2 Exchange and Efficiency

Exchange & Consumption Edgeworth Box

  • Setting
  • Edgeworth Box
  • Pareto Efficiency
  • Assumptions
  • Limitations
  • two goods (\(x\), \(y\)), two consumers (\(A\), \(B\)) with utility \[ u_A(x_A,y_A)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad u_B(x_B,y_B) \]
  • pure exchange economy: no money, no prices, no production
  • each consumer has endowment: \(E_A=(x_A^e,y_A^e)\) and \(E_B=(x_B^e,y_B^e)\)
  • fixed totals \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\)
  • consumers can trade \(x\) and \(y\) to improve their own utility
  • box limited by fixed totals \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\)
  • two indifference curve maps for \(A\) and \(B\) in single graph (IC map for \(B\) is flipped around, utility for \(B\) improves going south-west)
  • endowment \(E\) reflects both \(E_A\) and \(E_B\) and has 4(!) coordinates
  • fixed totals \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\) imply: \(E_B=(\bar x - x_A^e,\bar y - y_A^e)\)

Pareto Preferred

Allocation \({\cal A}\) is Pareto-preferred to \({\cal B}\) (Pareto superior) if, by moving from \({\cal B}\) to \({\cal A}\), at least one agent is strictly better off, and no agent loses (Pareto improvement).

  • i.o.w.: Pareto Improvement = mutual gains from trade can be realized

Pareto Efficient

Allocation \({\cal A}\) is Pareto efficient when there are no more Pareto preferred allocations.

  • if ICs differentiable & strictly convex to origin, Pareto efficient \(\Rightarrow\) \(MRS_A = MRS_B\)
  • ordinality and incomparability are minimally necessary assumptions on utilities for Pareto efficiency
  • utility levels have no intrinsic meaning, a higher number only indicates higher level of satisfaction: more is better
    • ordinal concept (order or ranking matters, not degree)
    • a utility number is not informative on absolute satisfaction or valuation
  • cannot compare utilities between individuals
    • two consumers could use different scales to measure their utility
  • Pareto efficiency is a very conservative welfare concept
    • extreme allocations (one agent has nothing, the other everything) are Pareto efficient
    • postulate of voluntary agreement to trade implies a single agent can have large power to block trades that benefit many others
    • two Pareto inefficient allocations cannot be ranked
    • no guidance what to choose in face of infinitely many Pareto efficient allocations

2.3 Production and Allocative Efficiency

Extension to Production

  • PPF
  • PPF Diagram
  • Allocative Efficiency
  • if \(x\) and \(y\) are produced, scarcity of inputs implies:
    • given input quantities, there is a trade-off between production of \(x\) and production of \(y\)
      • embodied in production possibilities frontier (PPF), or transformation frontier
    • if inputs become less scarce, production can expand
      • shifts out PPF, but can also enlarge Edgeworth box
  • PPF shows maximal amounts of \(x\) and \(y\) that can be produced with given input \(\bar z\)
  • (absolute) slope of PPF: marginal rate of transformation, MRT
  • embodies trade-off in production between \(x\) and \(y\)
  • Edgeworth box illustrates Pareto efficiency: \(MRS_A = MRS_B\)
  • PPF limits \(\bar x\) and \(\bar y\) and thus determines size of the box
  • allocative efficiency: \[MRS_A = MRS_B = MRT\]
  • consumers’ marginal willingness to give up \(y\) for \(x\) matches producers’ marginal transformation of \(y\) into \(x\)

2.4 Utility Possibilities and Social Welfare

Utility Possibilities Frontier (UPF)

  • UPF
  • Construction
  • Remark: Shape
  • now consider utility levels
  • what is the highest level of utility an individual can achieve, given \(\bar{x}\) and \(\bar{y}\), and allowing trade between agents?
  • this trade-off between agents’ utility levels is described by UPF
    • analysis is: given \(u_A\), what is \(\max u_B\)?
  • for convenience, normalize utilities \(\ge 0\) \(\to\) origin of graph at 0
  • with ordinal utilities, UPF will be ordinal
  • in a one-good model, the UPF can be constructed like shown with the PPF
  • in a two-goods model, the UPF can be constructed by tracing out utility levels when moving along the contract curve in an Edgeworth Box
  • all points on the UPF are Pareto efficient: utility of one agent can only be improved at the expense of the other’s utility
  • UPF need not be nicely concave as shown in this graph
  • shape of UPF may matter when society chooses one particular of the many Pareto efficient allocations
  • slope of UPF has no direct interpretation

First Theorem of Welfare Economics

Under strict conditions, a (general) competitive market equilibrium is Pareto efficient.

  • i.o.w.: market clearing \(\implies\) efficiency
  • ‘general’ refers to a complete set of markets
  • among the strict conditions, need perfect information for all agents, and absence of frictions

Social Welfare Function (SWF)

  • SWF
  • Relevance
  • Shape
  • Equity
  • Graph
  • Atkinson
  • First Best
  • SWF: society’s `utility’ function
  • Bergson-Samuelson SWF \(W()\): purely based on individual utilities \[ W=W(u^A,u^B)\]
  • as utilities depend on income or consumption, valuation through SWF reflects society’s stance on inequality
  • social decisions involve trade-off across groups or individuals
    • interpersonal comparability of utilities necessary
  • SWF is a modeling device, no direct observable counterpart in the real world
  • can conceive of a social planner (policymaker) as if their choices were guided by an SWF
    • compare: consumer of reacting to price and income changes as if she was maximizing utility
  • SWF used to balance off opposing interests / model trade-offs between groups
  • SWF can capture important dimensions of ‘broad well-being’
  • economics offers little guidance on ‘correct’ shape of the SWF
  • normative and philosophical considerations may play a role
  • that information has to come from other ways at aggregating individual preferences to a social preference structure
  • can conceive of a social choice mechanism that determines social preferences, e.g. 
    • democracy: electoral voting process
    • autocracy: preferences of a single individual (dictator) or of elite groups

Some Further Rationales for Redistribution

  • Rawls: minimax criterion as natural choice when deciding from ‘behind a veil of ignorance’, and focus on equality of outcomes
  • but: Nozick: society has no command over an income it can redistribute;
    • hence: rather focus on equality of opportunity
    • commodity egalitarianism
    • altruism
    • social stability

three canonical cases obtain for varying degrees of inequality aversion, \(\varepsilon\):

  1. \(\varepsilon=0\): society indifferent to inequality, Bentham or utilitarian
  2. \(0<\varepsilon<\infty\): society averse to inequality, Nash or convex case
  3. \(\varepsilon\to\infty\): society extremely inequality averse, Rawls or maximin case

The SWF depicted in the graph is due to Anthony Atkinson and reads \[ A_\varepsilon(u_A, u_B)= \begin{cases} 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(\frac{1}{2}\left(u_{A}^{1-\varepsilon}+u_{B}^{1-\varepsilon}\right)\right)^{1/(1-\varepsilon)} & \mbox{for}\ 0 \leq \epsilon \neq 1 \\ 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\left(u_A\cdot u_B\right)^{1/2} & \mbox{for}\ \varepsilon=1\\ 1-\frac{1}{\mu}\min\{u_A,u_B\} & \mbox{for}\ \varepsilon\to \infty \end{cases} \] where \(\varepsilon\) can take any value from 0 upwards and \(\mu\) is a constant.

This flexibility allows to consider a large range of (symmetric and convex) social indifference curves.

Similar to: CES indifference curves for a single consumer.

  • Combining SWF and UPF allows to determine the optimal social choice: First Best, \(F\).
  • \(F\) may differ when different SWF is applied
  • Technically, UPF acts as a constraint on SWF.
  • This way, we bring together Pareto efficiency (UPF) and society’s preference with respect to equity.

Second Theorem of Welfare Economics

Any Pareto efficient outcome can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium, for some distribution of initial endowments.

Caveat: This needs initial, non-distortionary lump-sum taxes and transfers before market interaction starts.

  • the process underlying the 2nd Theorem is called decentralization (achieving a social goal using decentral market allocation)
  • since the most-preferred (SWF-maximizing) allocation is achievable, equity and efficiency are, in fact, separable
  • lump-sum taxes and transfers are a theoretical benchmarking tool
  • they are tied to the identity of the tax payer or transfer recipient, but not to agents’ choices (e.g.: income, consumption, wealth) and can therefore not be influenced by individual agents

Summary

Summary

  • The marginal rate of substitution is a key measure to capture an individual’s willingness to trade (or give up, or pay)
  • Pareto efficiency is a key concept for societal welfare
    • can be illustrated with an Edgeworth box
    • embodies the exhaustion of gains from trade through voluntary action
    • to achieve it, one can rely on property rights and competitive markets (1st Theorem)
  • allocative efficiency relevant when including production
  • Pareto efficiency does not account for inequality concerns
  • social welfare function: trade-off between individual utilities, allows to reflect inequality aversion at the society level
  • first-best social choice combines the quest for efficiency with society’s views on equity
    • lump-sum taxes and transfers may be needed (2nd Theorem)
