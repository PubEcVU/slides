Utility Function and Indifference Curves

  • Utility Function
  • Ind’ Curve Maps
  • Ordinality and Incomparability
  • … Graphs
  • Notes
  • a utility function is a mathematical model for consumer preferences
  • with two goods, \(x\) and \(y\), we write \[u(x,y)\]
  • this is a three-dimensional object (two inputs, one output)
  • we specify \(u()\) for convenience, e.g. \[u = x + y \qquad \text{or} \qquad u = x \cdot y\] are easy to handle using algebra or calculus
  • two popular specifications (sporting a couple of parameters) that every econ student needs to know are
    • Cobb Douglas \[f(x,y)=x^{\alpha}\cdot y^{\left(1-\alpha\right)}\]
    • Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) \[f(x,y)=\left(ax^{\frac{s-1}{s}}+\left(1-a\right)y^{\frac{s-1}{s}}\right)^{\frac{s}{s-1}}\]
  • ICs are the 2D level curves of the 3D function \(u(x,y)\)
  • they can be obtained by horizontally ‘slicing’ the 3D function, keeping contant a vertical level of \(u\)
  • at any chosen level of \(u\), there is one indifference curve (IC)
  • slicing (infinitely) many times, gives (infinitely) many ICs (IC map)
  • for intuition, play with the 3D widget on the right
  • click on “> Utility” to change the functional form, e.g. try \(f\left(x,y\right)=\sqrt{x}+\sqrt{y}\)
  • the level of utility has no intrinsic meaning, except a higher number indicates a higher level of satisfaction or valuation: more is better
  • this is an ordinal concept (order or ranking matters, not degree)
  • from a utility number, we cannot say anything about the absolute satisfaction or valuation
  • also, we cannot compare utilities between individuals
    • two consumers could use different scales to measure their utility
  • the following graphs illustrate:
    • ordinality: the shape of indifference curves are unaffected when the scale parameter \(s\) is shifted
    • substitution: shifting the location \(a\) of the point performs a movement along a level curve

  • utility functions and indifference curves contain the same information, but \(u()\) is efficient mathematical notation, hence convenient
  • ordinality and incomparability will be dropped when we analyze economic inequality and social choice
  • preferences (utility functions, indifference curves) are assumed to be stable between choice situations that we compare
    • indifference curves never move in our models

  • utility focuses on the valuation of measurable quantities (consumption bundles)

Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)

  • Graph
  • Underlying Math
  • Math Examples
  • Notes
  • MRS is a fundamental concept to characterize consumer choice

MRS

  • tells how much a consumer is willing to give up in terms of units of \(y\) in return for getting an (infinitesimally small) unit of \(x\)
  • hence, measures willingness to give up, or willingness to pay
  • MRS (a positive number) equals (the negative of) the slope of the indifference curve

  • how does the \(u\)-value of \(u(x,y)\) change along an IC when we vary \(x\) and \(y\)?

  • the answer is: along an IC the change in \(u\), \(\text d u\), will be zero

  • using the formula for a total differential we can decompose this ‘zero change’ into changes due to either \(x\) or \(y\): \[ \text{d}u = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \text{d}x + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \text{d}y =0 \]

  • rearrangement (using simple algebra) leads to \[ \frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = -\color{blue}{\frac{{\partial u}/{\partial x}}{{\partial u}/{\partial y}}} \]

  • the slope of the IC, \({\text{d}y}/{\text{d}x}\), equals the negative of the MRS

\(u(x,y) = \ln(x)+\ln(y)\): \[MRS = \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{\frac{1}{y}}=\frac{y}{x},\]

\(u(x,y) = \sqrt{x} \cdot \sqrt{y}\): \[MRS = \frac{\frac{\sqrt{y}}{2\sqrt{x}}}{\frac{\sqrt{x}}{2\sqrt{y}}}=\frac{y}{x}.\]

  • MRS is a local concept: along a given IC, MRS typically changes with \(x\) and \(y\)

    • Get the hang of the MRS

      • move the dot in the graph to see how the MRS number changes
      • verify the shown MRS number by measuring the slope \({\text{d}y}/{\text{d}x}\) at the green dot
    • there are exceptions (which ones?) Hint: play with the IC maps
  • different utility functions can have the same MRS (see tab Math Examples)
    • this is because different utility functions may differ in the 3rd but not in the other dimensions (ordinal concept)

Budget Constraint (BC)

  • consumers have a fixed income \(m\) and they face prices \(p_x\) and \(p_y\)

  • we call \(\qquad p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y\qquad\) expenditure

  • expenditure cannot exceed income \(p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y\le m\)

  • under ‘more-is-better’ consumers spend it all: choose points \((y,x)\) on budget constraint \[y = \frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x\]

  • then maximize utility subject to the BC

  • in graph:

    • intersection points of BC with axes are \(\frac{m}{p_x}\) and \(\frac{m}{p_y}\)
    • slope is \(\displaystyle -\frac{p_x}{p_y}\)

Two Intuitive Ways to Maximize Utility

Substitute Out \(y\) using BC

  • we have 3 variables: \(x\), \(y\) and \(u\)
  • first get rid of one of them, for instance \(y\)
  • we use the budget constraint \[\quad\underbrace{\color{red}{y}=\frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x}_{\color{red}{\downarrow}}\]
  • and substitute into \(u(x,\color{red}{y})\)
  • then, we do simple maximization: \(\max_x u(x)\)

Via MRS = Slope of BC

  • we use the marginal condition for maximization \[\frac{MU_x(x,y)}{p_x} = \frac{MU_y(x,y)}{p_y} \quad \text{or} \quad MRS(x,y)=\frac{p_x}{p_y}\]
  • since there are (infinitely) many pairs of \((x,y)\) where this holds, we still need to combine with budget constraint \[\underbrace{\color{red}{y}=\frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{p_x}{p_y}\cdot x}_{\color{red}{\downarrow}}\]
  • and substitute into \(MRS(x,\color{red}{y})\)
  • then, we use algebra to solve for \(x\)

Which one’s easier? Depends on functional form.

Constrained Maximization: Lagrange Approach

  • Utility Maximization
  • Interpretation
  • Math Video\(^\star\)
  • Note
  • to use the Lagrange approach for constrained optimization, first, set up the Lagrange function with \(\lambda\) as multiplier

\[ {\cal L}(x,y,\lambda) = u(x,y) - \lambda \cdot (p_x \cdot x + p_y \cdot y-m) \]

  • then, set first derivatives w.r.t. \(x\), \(y\) and \(\lambda\) equal to zero (first order conditions, FOCs)

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial x} =\;\; \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial x} - \lambda \cdot p_x &=& 0 \\ \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial y} =\;\; \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial y} - \lambda \cdot p_y &=& 0 \\ \frac{\partial {\cal L} }{\partial \lambda} = m- p_x \cdot x - p_y \cdot y &=& 0 \end{aligned}\]

  • rewrite the first couple of equations and divide one by the other to eliminate \(\lambda\)

\[\left. \begin{array}{ccc} \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial x} &=& \lambda \cdot p_x \\ \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial y} &=& \lambda \cdot p_y \end{array} \right\} \to \frac{\frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial x}}{\frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial y}} = \frac{p_x}{p_y} \]

  • and realize that this is just the MRS equal to relative price condition (‘equal slopes’)
  • combine with the third FOC as before to solve for \(x\) and \(y\)

Exercise

Solve the Cobb Douglas case using Lagrange and find the optimal value of \(\lambda^\star\)

  • once solved, the optimal quantities \(x^\star\) and \(y^\star\) are functions of the parameters \((p_x,p_y,m)\) of the problem, and hence evaluating utility at that optimum gives a function \[u(x^\star, y^\star)=v(p_x,p_y,m)\]
  • \(v()\) is known in economics as indirect utility function, mathematically, it is a value function
  • in turn, from any of the first two FOCs, the optimal value of \(\lambda\) can be obtained as \(\displaystyle \lambda^\star =\left. \frac{\partial u(x^\star, y^\star)}{\partial x}\right/ p_x\)
  • \(\lambda^\star()\) is therefore also a function of the parameters
  • of particular interest is the dependence of \(v()\) and \(\lambda^\star()\) on \(m\); the derivative \(\displaystyle \frac{\text{d}v(m)}{\text{d}m}\) tells how the maximally attainable level of utility changes (increases) if we marginally change (relax) the constraint (give the consumer one dollar more)
  • this turns out to be \(\displaystyle \frac{\text{d}v(m)}{\text{d}m} = \lambda^\star(m)\)
  • so, \(\lambda\) can be interpreted as the shadow value or shadow price of the constraint

Advanced math video clearly showing the intuition for optimizing a parabolic function \(f(x,y)\) subject to an elliptical constraint \(g(x,y)=k\) and making clear how the Lagrange method works.

The clue is that (vector-valued) derivatives of \(f(x,y)\) and \(g(x,y)\) are parallel in the optimum, and \(\lambda\) is used as weight to make them equal. Parallel derivatives imply ‘equal slopes’.

In our application,

\(f(x,y) = u(x,y)\), \(\quad g(x,y)=p_x \cdot x + p_y \cdot y\quad\), \(k=m\)

\[ \left. \begin{array}{ccc} \nabla f(x,y) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial u(x,y)}{\partial y} \end{array}\right) \\ \nabla g(x,y) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} p_x \\ p_y \end{array}\right) \end{array} \right\} \stackrel{FOC}{\Rightarrow} \begin{array}{ccc} \nabla f(x,y) &=& \lambda \cdot \nabla g(x,y)\\ m&=&g(x,y) \end{array} \]

  • the Lagrange approach is quite general and can be extended to any number of \(n\) variables and \(k\) constraints
  • it equally works for minimization
  • it can be extended to incorporate inequality constraints (Kuhn Tucker)

Straight Line Demand

For simplicity and convenience only, demand functions are often depicted as/approximated by straight lines.

  • In a two-goods model, it is more accurate to write \[ \begin{split} x &= D_x(p_x,p_y,m) \\ y &= D_y(p_x,p_y,m) \\ \end{split} \] making clear that all quantities can depend on all prices and incomes
  • for the square-root Cobb Douglas utility, it is easy to show that \[ \begin{split} D_x(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_x)\\ D_y(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_y) \end{split} \] i.e., demands do not depend directly on the price of the other good.
  • for the parameterized Cobb Douglas utility \(u(x,y)=x^{\alpha} \cdot y^{1-\alpha}\) start with \(MRS=p_x/p_y\) and then incorporate \(y=m/p_y-x\cdot p_x/p_y\) from the BC
  • marginal utility of \(x\) is \(\displaystyle \qquad\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \alpha\cdot x^{\alpha-1} \cdot y^{1-\alpha} = \alpha\cdot x^{\alpha} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \cdot y^{1-\alpha} = \frac{\alpha}{x} u(x,y)\)
  • analogously, \({\partial u}/{\partial y}= {(1-\alpha) u(x,y)}/{y}\) so that \[MRS = \frac{{\partial u}/{\partial x}}{{\partial u}/{\partial y}} = \frac{\alpha y}{(1-\alpha)x} = \frac{p_x}{p_y}\]
  • rearrange, and substitute \(y\) from the BC to find \(\displaystyle \qquad\frac{m}{p_y}-\frac{x\cdot p_x}{p_y} = \frac{(1-\alpha)p_x x}{\alpha p_y}\)
  • rearrange \[m/p_y = \left(\frac{(1-\alpha)p_x}{\alpha p_y} + \frac{p_x}{p_y}\right)x\] multiply with \(p_y\) \[m = \left(\frac{(1-\alpha)p_x}{\alpha} + \frac{\alpha p_x}{\alpha}\right)x\] simplify \[m = \left(\frac{p_x}{\alpha}\right)x\]
  • and finally, \[x = D_x(p_x,p_y,m)=\frac{\alpha}{p_x}m\]
  • analogously, \[y = D_y(p_x,p_y,m)=\frac{1-\alpha}{p_y}m\]
  • test: square-root utility obtains for \(\alpha=1/2\) \[ \begin{split} D_x(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_x)\\ D_y(p_x,p_y,m) &= m/(2p_y) \end{split} \]

Engel Curve

  • how does demand change when income is changed (or varies in the population)?
  • once we have done the optimization, we just reinterpret:
    • the quantity from the mathematical demand function \(x=D_x(p_x,p_y,m)\) can can also be interpreted as a function of income with \(p_x\) and \(p_y\) as fixed parameters \[x=D(m)\]
    • same math, different picture (typically \(m\) on the horizontal axis and \(x\) on the vertical), called Engel Curve

Engel’s Law

  • empirical regularity showing that the food share in household budgets decreases with income
  • households with low incomes spend absolutely less on food than households with middle or high income, low earners spend relatively more
  • reason: food consumption is one of the first needs to address

Elasticity

  • Definition
  • Demand and Supply Elasticities
  • Core Warnings
  • by how many percentage points does a function value change, when the argument changes by 1%
  • elasticities are unit free
  • elasticities are evaluated locally at a point
  • equivalent definitions
  1. \(\displaystyle \eta(x) = f'(x) \cdot \frac{x}{f(x)}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \eta(x) = f'(x) \left/ \frac{f(x)}{x} \right. \qquad\) (slope of function at a point divided by slope of ray through origin to that point)
  3. \(\displaystyle \eta(x) = \frac{f'(x)}{x'} \left/ \frac{f(x)}{x} \right. \qquad\) (note: \(x'=1\))
  4. \(\displaystyle \eta(x) = \frac{\text d \ln(f(x))}{\text d \ln(x)}.\)

  • There are two effects on \(x\) from an increase in \(p_x\)

Measuring Consumer Welfare

  • Change of Consumer Surplus
  • Graph
  • a common welfare measure is the change of consumer surplus
  • this can be illustrated as the “area under the demand curve”
    • “under” in our picture with price on the vertical axis means to the left of the demand curve, since we “integrate” over price
    • typically, we fix a baseline price \(p_x^\circ\) and consider a price change, so we measure the change of consumer surplus as we change the price to \(p_x^\prime\): \[\Delta CS = \int_{p_x^\circ}^{p_x^\prime} D_x(p_x; p_y, m) \text{d} p_x\]
  • this graph illustrates the change of consumer surplus for the Cobb Douglas case
    • shift the dot to change the price of \(x\)
    • this pivots the budget constraint around \(m/p_y\) on the vertical axis
    • a price increase (decrease) is associated with a loss (gain) of consumer surplus = red (green)
    • this change reflects both substitution and income effects

Endowment

  • Budget Constraint
  • Graph
  • Labor Supply
  • Intertemporal Choice
  • consumers may be equipped with goods that they can sell: endowment
  • make two assumptions:
    1. those goods are the same goods consumers could also buy
    2. prices for buying or selling are the same
    • picture changes, but analytical model unchanged
  • denote endowment quantities \(x^e\) and \(y^e\): these are parameters; BC changes to \[ \begin{split} \overbrace{p_x\cdot x+p_y\cdot y}^{\text{value of purchases}} &= \overbrace{m+p_x\cdot x^e+p_y\cdot y^e}^{\text{value of income and phys. endowment}} \\ \underbrace{p_x\cdot (x-x^e)+p_y\cdot (y-y^e)}_{\text{net purchase}} &= m. \end{split} \]
  • consumer will sell some of the good she has plenty of (compared to the desired optimal allocation)
  • consumer will buy more of the good she has little of

Observe!

You can shift the endowment point along the BC. A shift implies, however, adjusting the money income accordingly. Endowment and money income are perfect substitutes in the graph. Holding \(m\) constant would imply the entire BC to shift with the endowment.

  • allocate time endowment \(\bar H\) between either hours of work \(h\) (to the left of horizontal axis), or leisure \(\ell\) (right)
  • vertical axis: (physical) consumption good \(c\) with price 1, or income
  • indifference curve: consumer likes \(c\) and \(\ell\) (not \(h\)), more of each is better
  • budget constraint: if income at \(h=0\) (or \(\ell = \bar H\)) is zero, then BC increases from point (\(\bar H, 0\)) to the left at rate \(w\) (hourly wage rate)
  • change in \(w\) has income and substitution effect on labor supply
  • at some level of \(w\), could get \(h\downarrow\)
  • allocate consumption over time \(t=0,1\): consume either today (\(c_0\), horizontal axis), or tomorrow (\(c_1\))
  • indifference curve: consumer likes both \(c_0\) and \(c_1\), more of each is better
  • budget constraint: endowment point determined by income today (\(m_0\)) and tomorrow (\(m_1\)), slope equals \(1+r\) (\(r\): common interest rate on both saving and borrowing)
    • if \(r_{borrow}>r_{lend}\) (credit costs more than saving returns), get a kink at the income endowment point
  • changing \(r\) has income and substitution effects on the intertemporal consumption decision
    • this swivels budget constraint around income endowment point (reload page to keep fixed)
    • often, \(r\uparrow\) means consume less today but save and consume more tomorrow

Summary

  • Choices are determined by preferences & constraints
  • Preferences can be illustrated using indifference curves, which in turn are derived from a number of axioms
  • Utility functions are a mathematical convenience tool to represent preferences, with ICs as their level curves
  • Utility maximization subject to a budget constraint leads to demand for goods
  • The demand function shows quantity demanded in dependence of prices and incomes
  • Prices and incomes can be influenced by policy (e.g., taxes and transfers)
  • Price variation can be used to derive individual welfare measures such as change in consumer surplus
  • labor supply and intertemporal choice can best be understood in the framework of consuming with an endowment

0.2 Aggregation and Market Demand

Many Consumers

  • classical macroeconomics assumes that aggregate economic relationships can be modeled as if they were driven by decisions of a ‘representative consumer’, some middle-of-the-road guy whose behavior is very similar to everybody else’s
  • modern macroeconomics explictly takes heterogeneous agents into account
  • the behavior of such diverse individuals needs to be aggregated; they differ
    • in terms of incomes
    • in terms of preferences
  • prices may be the same for everybody (we assume)

Preference Heterogeneity

  • in the real world, most consumers are different from one another, their tastes or preferences are not identical but heterogeneous
  • in our model, we can take this into account in a parameterized Cobb Douglas utility by making the preference parameter \(\alpha_i\) specific for an individual \(i=1,\ldots,N\): \[u_i(x,y)=x^{\alpha_i} \cdot y^{1-\alpha_i}\]

Example

  • \(N=5\) and a uniform distribution of \(\alpha\): \(0.1\), \(0.3\), \(0.5\), \(0.7\), \(0.9\), implies a uniform distribution of budget shares for good \(x\):
    \(\qquad 10\%, 30\%, 50\%, 70\%, 90\%\)
  • if there is a negative correlation with income, and income is likewise uniformly distributed, e.g.
    \(\qquad 250, 200, 150, 100, 50\) euros, then consumers spend
    \(\qquad 25, 60, 75, 70, 45\) euros on \(x\)—this is not uniform anymore!

Horizontal Aggregation

  • denote quantity demanded by consumer \(i\) as \(x_i=D_i(p)\), then aggregate or market demand is \[X = D(p) = \sum_i^N D_i(p)\]
  • graphically, we aggregate horizontally (price on the vertical axis and identical for all consumers)
  • market demand curve will be flatter than individual curves

Kinks

  • horizontally aggregating linear individual demand curves may lead to a piece-wise linear aggregate demand curve with kinks
  • this happens when the vertical intercept differs between individuals

Aggregation and Income Distribution

  • if there is no preference heterogeneity it may be tempting to write \[X = D(p,\overline{m})\] where \(\overline{m}\) is average income in the country (or market), or some other aggregate (like GDP per capita)
  • however, doing the exact aggregation \[X = \sum_i^N D_i(p_x, p_y, m_i)\] shows that market demand depends through the individual demand functions on all individual incomes and hence on the entire income distribution
  • if individual demand functions are nonlinear in \(m_i\), it is unlikely that average income is a good proxy for the actual income distribution

Summary

  • prices are determined at the market level, so individual demands must be aggregated across consumers, given the price
  • in a typical diagram with price on the vertical axis, the aggregation is “horizontal” (adding up quantities, given the price)
  • with heterogeneous incomes, aggregate demand tends to depend on the entire income distribution
  • with heterogeneous preferences, aggregating from the micro level may become technically involved

0.3 Single Competitive Firm

Technology

  • Production Function
  • Production Possibilities
  • a firm’s production technology relates input quantity \(z\) to maximally feasible output quantity \(q=f(z)\)
  • its derivative is the marginal product or marginal productivity \(MP=f'(z)\)
  • the concave case is often studied and refers to decreasing returns to scale
  • in case there are two inputs, the left panel shows \(q=f(z_1, z_2=\text{low})\) and \(q=f(z_1, z_2=\text{high})\) (dotted)
  • the production possibilities frontier (PPF) shows what an economy can maximally produce in terms of multiple outputs (say, \(x\) and \(y\)) when the amount of available input is limited to \(\bar z\), and a choice needs to be made whether to use a given input unit in \(x\) or in \(y\): \(z_x\) or \(z_y\)
  • the (negative of the) slope of the PPF is called the marginal rate of transformation, MRT

Cost Function

  • Single Input
  • Marginal Cost
  • Fixed Cost
  • Average Cost
  • Notes
  • firm employs labor \(z\) (hours) at wage rate \(w\) (per hour), then dollar outlays are \(w\cdot z\)
  • invert production function to substitute out \(z\) for a function of \(q\): \[q=f(z)\quad \Longrightarrow \quad z=f^{-1}(q)\equiv g(q)\] and plug it into outlays to get the cost of output function \(C(q)\): \[w\cdot z = w\cdot g(q) \equiv C(q).\]
  • i.o.w. the cost of output function is the scaled inverse of the production function

Example

\[w=3\] \[q = 2\sqrt{z}\] \[g(q) = \frac{q^2}{4}\] \[C(q) = \frac{3}{4} \cdot q^2 \]

  • marginal change in cost when producing additional output \[MC(q)=\frac{\mathrm{d}C(q)}{\mathrm{d}q} = C'(q).\]

  • in this simple 1-input case, MC is proportional to inverse of marginal productivity MP \[MC=\frac{w}{MP}\]

  • \(F\): cost components that the firm cannot influence through variation of inputs
  • 2-input case: firm can only change labor, and has to take the level of capital as given: the associated outlays (for capital) are fixed
  • outlays associated with the variable input will give rise to variable cost, \(VC(q)\), that vary with the production level

  • ratio of total production cost at production level \(q\) in relation to that production level: \[AC(q)=\frac{C(q)}{q} = \underbrace{\frac{F}{q}}_{AFC} +\underbrace{\frac{V(q)}{q}}_{AVC}\]

  • \(F/q\) is hyperbolic

  • if \(VC(q)\) is linear in output \[VC(q)=b\cdot q,\qquad AVC=b=MC\]

  • \(\min_q AC(q)\to q^{mes}\) is minimum efficient scale

  • similar to a consumer’s substitution between ingredients of a consumption bundle, a firm may use several inputs in production, and find that it can substitute between them to achieve the same level of output at different input combinations
  • firm would first try to find the optimal combination of inputs that, at given input prices, delivers the target level of output at the smallest expense
  • under changing relative factor prices, input substitution can deliver additional cost saving
  • total cost \(C(q)\) is then lower than if one or more inputs were kept fixed

Competitive Firm

  • Profit
  • Profit Max
  • Supply
  • Graph
  • goal of any firm is to make money
  • profit \(\Pi(q)\) is difference between sales revenue \(R(q)\) and cost \(C(q)\)
  • profit should not go negative, else the firm better stops: \(\Pi(q) \ge 0\)
  • production should not exceed demand \(q \le D(p)\)
  • firm is constrained by its technology, which is given (\(C(q)\) reflects to an important degree the production function)
  • first order condition of profit maximization implicitly defines the optimal \(q\) \[MR(q)-MC(q)=0.\]

  • marginal revenue \(MR(q)\) equals \(p\): the firm takes \(p\) as given, but is unrestricted by demand: the firm can supply infinitely much

  • profit maximization implies \[R(q) = p\cdot q \quad \Longrightarrow \quad MR(q) = \frac{\text{d}R(q)}{\text{d}q} = p \quad \Longrightarrow \quad p-MC(q)=0.\]

  • implies marginal cost pricing: firm adjusts \(q\) such that MC equals \(p\)

  • note: \(q\) is only chosen if price covers average cost and firm breaks even, \(\Pi\ge 0\) \[ \Pi = p\cdot q - C(q)\ge 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad p\ge \frac{C(q)}{q} = AC(q), \]

  • market price \(p\) is given parameter
  • hence, competitive firm’s supply function \[q=S(p)\] depends on the price
  • firm chooses \(q\) such that its \(MC\) equals the given market price, under the condition that \(q\) exceeds its minimum efficient scale \(q^{mes}\)
  • supply function is the upward-sloping part of MC function as long as \(p\ge AC(q)\)
  • \(MC\) intersects \(AC\) curve in its minimum at \(q^{mes}\)
  • since \(p\) is given (competitive firm), and firm expands production until the increasing \(MC\) curve equals \(p\), profit equals the orange reactangle

Play!

  • simulate a price change by shifting the given market price \(p\)

Summary

  • competitive firms that take prices as given and choose a profit maximizing quantity are at the heart of the competitive model that brings maximal welfare to consumers
  • important for understanding the output decision are production technology and various cost concepts that derive from it
  • firms cease to exist (exit the market) if economic profits turn (structurally) negative, since an economic profit of zero is the outside option that firm owners can get by putting their resources into other activities

Surplus and Welfare

  • Definitions
  • Graph
  • consumer surplus: difference between marginal willingness to pay and price paid
    • to be aggregated across all units/consumers/producers in the market
  • similar: producer surplus as firm’s value of economic activity (disregarding fixed cost)
    • or: difference between minimum necessary compensation for production (marginal cost) and price obtained per unit
  • ‘welfare’: efficient use of available but scarce resources
  • measure of (Paretian) welfare: sum of producer and consumer surplus
  • competitive market with undistorted flexible prices leads to maximal (Paretian) welfare \(:=\) the benchmark
  • competitive markets maximize surplus
  • even when demand (or supply) conditions change

Distortions and Welfare (Deadweight) Loss

  • Issue
  • Graph
  • Calculating the Triangle
  • distortions (taxes, quotas, regulation, etc.) lead to deviations from the benchmark \(:=\) welfare loss or deadweight loss (DWL)
  • the source of the deadweight loss is that at the resulting (or allowed) price, the quantities supplied and demanded diverge, and the shorter side of the market determines the quantity actually traded
  • in other words: at too low a quantity, consumers are willing to pay more than it would cost the producer to supply those extra units
  • in other words: welfare loss results from units of quantity not produced (or, traded), even though fundamental economic relations (the cost and demand structure) would allow that production to take place
  • gov’t fixes price at \(p_0\), equilibrium quantity \(Q_0\) results (shorter side of the market)
  • distortions to competitive price adjustment can leave the equilibrium point at \(F\), associated with a deadweight loss
  • even when demand (or supply) conditions change

\[\begin{aligned} Q = D(p) &=& 7.5 - p\\ Q = S(p) &=& -0.5+p. \end{aligned}\] equilibrium: \[Q^\star = 3.5 \qquad p^\star = 4.\]

If actual quantity traded is \(Q_0 = 1\), welfare loss triangle is \[\frac{1}{2} \cdot \underbrace{(Q^\star-Q_0)}_{b} \times \underbrace{(p_0 - p_0')}_{h} = 0.5 \cdot 2.5 \cdot 5 = 6.25.\]

Summary

  • aggregated market demand (across consumers) and market supply (across firms) determine the equilbrium price and quantities in the market
  • from here, aggregate welfare measures such as consumer surplus and producer surplus can be determined
  • any deviation from a total-surplus-maximizing competitive equilbrium hence triggers a welfare loss (aka deadweight loss)

0.5 Game Theory: Two Players

Game Theory

  • Game theory offers tools of analysis of strategy and conflict, and naturally finds numerous applications in modeling economic and political decisions
  • there are two main branches:
    • non-cooperative game theory
    • cooperative game theory
  • cooperative game theory deals with actors cooperating inside a group (coalition, alliance)
    and agreeing to split a slice of cake, and competing as a group with others about who gets what slice;
    inside the group, binding agreements are possible
  • here: focus on non-cooperative games

Non-Cooperative Game Theory

  • strategic interaction of rational (and typically selfish) players
  • rules of the game and rationality of players are common knowledge
  • common knowledge: player 1 knows that player 2 knows that player 1 knows …
  • noncooperative game theory: players max private pay-offs
    but take into account the effect of other players’ choices on their own pay-offs
  • no direct cooperation, no formal means of enforcing cooperative behavior (no binding agreements)
  • this does not mean that in a noncooperative game players cannot cooperate; they can do so implicitly

Dominant Strategies

  • payoffs per player depend on actions chosen by both players
  • they move simultaneously and do not talk, cooperate, or commit
  • no matter what US chooses: China has highest personal payoff from playing Pollute (dominant strategy)
  • symmetry: same for US
  • upshot: equilibrium in dominant strategies
    • two selfish, rational and independent players have no reason to deviate from polluting
    • dilemma: coordination would have led to overall improvement
  • game is known as Prisoners’ Dilemma

Example: US and China Pollution Game

Weak Dominance

action \({\cal B}\) weakly dominates \({\cal A}\) if

  1. \({\cal B}\)’s payoff is at least as high \({\cal A}\)’s— irrespective of the opponent’s choice; and

  • Pollute is weakly dominant for China, but (Abate,Abate) is not an equilibrium

Further Remarks

  • many (or most) games do not have a dominant strategy (equilibrium)
  • equilibria in strictly dominant strategies are always also Nash equilibria
  • Nash equilibria may not be unique

  • often, as elsewhere in economics, we assume self-interested rational players that maximizing their own payoffs

  • the main equilibrium concept in game theory is that of a Nash equilibrium, where everyone plays the best response to everybody else

  • the Prisoner’s Dilemma is a special case where players have dominant strategies

Duopoly: Quantities

  • Issue
  • Math Example
  • \(\ldots\)cont’d.
  • Graph
  • Isoprofit Contours
  • Oligopoly
  • 2 firms (\(A\), \(B\)) in the market, constant MC, no fixed cost, both produce the same, identical good
  • consumers are indifferent to buying at \(A\) or \(B\)
  • if firms each choose their quantity, they will take into account that
    • the other firm makes choices (so \(A\) reacts to \(B\), as in a game with Nash equilibrium strategies)
    • the price falls if they together produce more (falling demand schedule)
  • suppose, market demand \(Q = q_A + q_B = 10-p\)
  • marginal cost \(\mathit{MC}_A=\mathit{MC}_B = 3\)
  • demand for firm \(A\): \(\displaystyle \qquad p_A(q_A, q_B) = 10 - q_A - q_B\)
  • marginal revenue (‘twice as steep’ as demand) equal to MC (profit max condition) \(\mathit{MR}_A = 10 - 2 q_A - q_B = \mathit{MC}_A\)
  • reaction function: \(\displaystyle 1 - 2 q_A - q_B = 3 \qquad \to \qquad q_A = \frac{1}{2}(7 - q_B)\)
  • similar for \(B\), and solve [\(q_A\) as function of \(q_B\), etc.] \[q_A(q_B) = \frac{1}{2} ({7 - q_B})\quad \text{and} \quad q_B(q_A) = \frac{1}{2} ({7 - q_A})\]
  • equilibrium: \(q_A = q_A (q_B) = q_A (q_B (q_A))\) \[q_A = \frac{1}{2} \left[{7 - \frac{1}{2} ({7 - q_A})} \right]\]
  • final solution for eq. quantities \(\displaystyle \qquad q_A^d = 2\frac{1}{3} \quad \text{and} \quad q_B^d = 2\frac{1}{3}\)
  • identical firms have identical market shares & profits
    • (ttl) market quantity, \(\displaystyle Q^d = 4\frac{2}{3}\)
    • market price, \(\displaystyle p^d = 5\frac{1}{3} > MC_i = 3,\; i=A,B\)
    • profits, \(\Pi_i = 5\frac{4}{9},\; i=A,B\)
  • reaction function (or best response function) is the optimal quantity decision of a firm given the competitor’s quantity
  • Nash equilibrium is where both reaction functions cross, or where best reponses are mutually compatible
  • equilibrium will react to, among others, changes in marginal cost
  • quantities are so-called strategic substitutes (\(A\) responds to \(B\)’s quantity change in the opposite direction), reaction functions slope downwards

  • perfect competition is welfare maximizing (Pareto efficient), price equals marginal cost

    • policy response: kindle competition, encourage free entry
  • Cournot duopoly outcome is in between competition and monopoly (in terms of price, welfare and quantity)
