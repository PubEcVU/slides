3.2 Efficiency & Pricing

Optimal Provision of Public Goods

  • Demand Aggregation
  • Lindahl Pricing
  • demand aggregation across consumers
    • private good: horizontal
    • public good: vertical
      • due to non-rivalry
  • two individuals \(A, B\) and production (\(MC\))
    • private good \(x\): \(\mathit{MC}_{x} = \mathit{MB}_{x}^A = \mathit{MB}_{x}^B\)
    • public good \(G\): \(\mathit{MC}_{G} = \mathit{MB}_{G}^A + \mathit{MB}_{G}^B\)
      • simplified form of Samuelson Rule
  • Lindahl’s question: what prices would support the efficient solution \(G^\star\)?
  • with heterogeneous preferences, prices or financing shares (taxes) may have to vary across consumers: \(\tau_i, \; i=1,2,\ldots\) so that \[G_i^\star(\tau_i)=G^\star = \text{const.}\]
  • \(\{\tau_1^\star, \tau_2^\star, \ldots \}\): optimal heterogeneous financing shares aka Lindahl prices
  • could be found by pricemaker calling out sets of prices until all consumers agree
  • i.e., the Lindahl result may come about as voting equilibrium

Allocative Efficiency: Public & Private Good

  • agent \(A\)’s problem (private \(x\) and public good \(G\)): \[ \begin{eqnarray*} \max_{x,G} \quad u_A(x,G) \\ \mathrm{s.t.} \quad u_B(x,G) &=&\bar{u}_B \\ F(x,G) &=& 0 \end{eqnarray*} \]

  • constraints:

    • \(F(x,G)=0\): keep allocations on the PPF
    • keep \(B\) on reservation utility \(\bar{u}_B\)

Samuelson Condition

\[ MRS_A(G,x) + MRS_B(G,x) = MRT(G,x) \tag{1}\]

3.3 Free Riding or Cooperation?

Social Dilemmas

  • Overview
  • Inaction, I
  • Inaction, II
  • public good provision is akin to social dilemmas known from other social sciences (bystander effect, diffusion of responsibility, tragedy of the commons)
  • in this context, inaction is individually rational: action means cost of effort for yourself and sharing results, inaction means no cost of effort and also sharing results
  • free riding: fundamental problem to rely on voluntary action of others
  • prisoner’s dilemma: equilibrium (market outcome) may result in inaction of all players, which is socially inefficient (underprovision and collective irrationality)

3.4 Mechanisms and Voting

Main Issue to Address

  • the Lindahl result may come about as voting equilibrium

  • individuals voluntarily choose the efficient amount, after having revealed their willingness-to-pay schedule

  • both market and Lindahl solution fail to be optimal because of non-excludability: players have incentive to misrepresent their preferences for public goods if contributions are linked to stated preferences for those goods

  • after all, players can consume (an inefficiently large amount) of the good ‘for free’

  • main informational problem: social planner (gov’t) does not know true preferences of people

  • alternatives

    1. remove non-excludability: turn public into club good

    2. preference revelation mechanism

    3. vote over budget and finance public good from general tax funds

Simplified Preference Revelation Mechanism

  • Intro
  • Example
  • (cont’d.)
  • Extension
  • mechanism design
    • devise incentive scheme that makes it in individuals’ best interest to reveal their true preferences
  • main idea: individuals are being charged according to their influencing the social decision whether or not to buy the good
  • we show a very simple example of a mechanism due to Vickrey, Groves & Clarke, VGC
    • there are a number of related truth-telling mechanisms offering alternatives and refinements
  • government may buy and pay for a public good of fixed size, and charge the players according to what they say
  • total cost \(10\), 2 players, true valuation per player \(8\)
    • total valuation \(\ge\) total cost, project should proceed
  • players report either ‘8’ or ‘0’ (i.e. tell truth or lie)
  • player is pivotal (influential) if his report changes the social decision
  • if I’m pivotal,
    and if there’s a possibility that we jointly could afford to buy the good, ‘I pay the full cost minus what you report’ (else I don’t pay)
  • thus: my payment does not depend on my own report

  • outcome: truth-telling weakly dominant strategy for both
  • problem: total payments received 4 \(<\) total cost \(10\)
  • one extension concerns notation and number of players
  • let there be \(N\) players, denote total cost by \(C\),
    individual true valuations by \(v_i\) and individual reports by \(r_i\)
  • project is socially desireable if \(C \le \sum_{i=1}^N v_i\)
  • project will be implemented if \(C \le \sum_{i=1}^N r_i\)
  • pivotal player \(j\) pays \(t_j = C - \sum_{i\neq j}^N r_i\), non-pivotal player pays \(t_j=0\)
  • net payoffs are then \(v_i-t_i \quad \forall i\)

Vote over Size of Public Good

  • Median Voter Theorem
  • Illustration
  • Outcome
  • let citizens vote over size of public good \(G\) (or size of gov’t), knowing fully well that more spending on it implies less spending on other (private) goods
  • different people will make different choices \(\to\) distribution of preferred choices

Median Voter Theorem

In political equilibrium (election), the proposal that coincides with the preference of the median voter determines the size of the public good that will be chosen (since it gets at least 50% support of all votes).

  • single-peaked individual prefereces






  • determination of median voter position

  • politically determined size \(G^{MV}\) may be larger or smaller than, or even coincide with, the optimal size \(G^\star\)
  • stable distribution of preferences \(\to\) stability of voting outcome
  • MV result not subject to strategic manipulation

Conclusion

Summary

  • public goods are important features of complex societies, and first-order relevant for the organization of gov’t (e.g., legal system, law enforcement, defense)
  • allocative efficiency characterized by Samuelson condition \[\sum_i MRS_i(G,x) = MRT(G,x)\]
  • Lindahl pricing under heterogeneous willingness to pay implies optimal financing shares that are differentiated between consumers
  • market outcomes often means underprovision relative to the efficient level
  • core difficulty for non-cooperative solutions to public good allocation problems
    • economic theory: mechanism design
  • voting (+ tax financing) as practical solution
